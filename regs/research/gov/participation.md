## 0. The machinery in one paragraph (context for the playbook)

- Tax policy is made by **HM Treasury** (policy + Budget decisions) and delivered/administered by **HMRC** (which also runs most technical consultations). The government's current statement of how it makes tax policy is the **"Tax Policy Making Principles"** policy paper, published by HM Treasury on **12 June 2025**. It commits to: a **single major fiscal event per year** ("predictable and stable framework"), "smart and agile consultation" ("engage stakeholders fully and flexibly", formal consultations "targeted and precise"), transparency on rationales and impacts, and swift delivery. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/tax-policy-making-principles (fetched 2026-07-06).
- The predecessor document, the **Tax Consultation Framework** (published 1 March 2011), was **withdrawn on 1 October 2025** as outdated and now points to the Tax Policy Making Principles. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/tax-consultation-framework (fetched 2026-07-06).
- Ministerial lead on tax (role-anchored): the **Exchequer Secretary to the Treasury** is currently the Treasury minister responsible for "the UK tax system including direct, indirect, business, property, and personal taxation", "tax administration policy", the Finance Bill, and is "departmental Minister for HM Revenue and Customs". As of 2026-07-06 the holder is **Daniel Tomlinson MP** (appointed 1 September 2025). Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/ministers/exchequer-secretary-to-the-treasury (fetched 2026-07-06). CAUTION: which junior Treasury minister holds the tax brief moves around between FST/XST across reshuffles — historically the **Financial Secretary to the Treasury** led on tax (e.g. Mel Stride 2017, Lucy Frazer 2021, Victoria Atkins 2022 all announced MTD changes as FST). As of 2026-07-06 the FST page (holder: Lord Livermore, appointed 8 July 2024) lists growth/skills/regional briefs and **no tax responsibilities**. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/ministers/financial-secretary-to-the-treasury (fetched 2026-07-06). For public content: say "the Treasury minister responsible for tax (currently the Exchequer Secretary)" and link the role page, not the person.

**Honest reading:** MTD was delayed/reshaped at least four times (2017, 2021, 2022, 2023-design), and every single climbdown announcement *names* the pressure sources: select committees, professional bodies, business groups. The levers that worked were **evidence of cost and unreadiness**, delivered through (a) Lords/Commons committee reports, (b) NAO/PAC value-for-money scrutiny, (c) professional-body consultation responses. No petition or letter-writing campaign appears in any of the government's own accounts. And the 2025 £20k extension shows the pressure valve swings back when attention moves on.

### 1b. The £10k→£30k→£50k threshold rethink (Dec 2022 + Nov 2023)

- Until 19 Dec 2022 the plan was mandation at **£10,000** income. The Dec 2022 announcement raised the entry threshold to **£50,000** (2026) and **£30,000** (2027), and launched a formal review for under-£30k. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/news/government-announces-phased-mandation-of-making-tax-digital-for-itsa (fetched 2026-07-06).
- The review concluded at Autumn Statement 2023 (22 Nov 2023): under-£30k mandation "kept under review", plus the design simplifications listed above. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/outcome-of-the-making-tax-digital-small-business-review (fetched 2026-07-06).
- Then Spring Statement 2025 set **£20,000 from April 2028** (~970,000 more people). Source: gov.uk policy paper above (fetched 2026-07-06). Lesson for readers: thresholds are the most movable part of MTD — and they move in both directions.

### 1c. Loan charge — two independent reviews forced by parliamentary pressure

**Round 1 (2019 Morse review):**
- Commissioned by the Chancellor **September 2019**; **Sir Amyas Morse** (former NAO head) reported **December 2019**; government accepted **19 recommendations**. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/disguised-remuneration-independent-loan-charge-review (fetched 2026-07-06; page first published 11 Sep 2019, last updated 26 Nov 2025).
- Concrete changes (HMRC guidance, exact wording): "the loan charge will apply only to outstanding loans made on, or after, **9 December 2010**"; loans before **6 April 2016** excluded "where a reasonable disclosure of the use of the tax avoidance scheme was made to HMRC and HMRC did not take action"; option "to spread the amount of their outstanding loan balance (as at 5 April 2019) evenly across 3 tax years"; **refunds of voluntary restitution** for settlements since March 2016 in years now out of scope. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/disguised-remuneration-independent-loan-charge-review/guidance (fetched 2026-07-06).
- HMRC's implementation report (Dec 2020): https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/independent-loan-charge-review-hmrc-report-on-implementation (URL verified in gov.uk search 2026-07-06).
- Who pushed: the **Loan Charge APPG** (cross-party MPs/peers) — its own site records "over 150 Parliamentarians" signing a letter to the PM and Chancellor, an APPG inquiry with reports (April 2019, November 2019), and evidence calls to affected individuals. The APPG "is staffed and funded by the Loan Charge Action Group" (their disclaimer — note this honestly). Source: https://www.loanchargeappg.co.uk/ (fetched 2026-07-06). MEDIUM confidence on attribution weight; HIGH that the APPG exists and did these things.

**Round 2 (2025 McCann review):**
- Commissioned at Autumn Budget 2024; announced **23 January 2025** by Written Ministerial Statement of the Exchequer Secretary; led by **Ray McCann**, former President of the CIOT. Call for evidence 28 Mar – 30 May 2025. **Final report published 26 November 2025** (Budget 2025) with the government response. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/independent-review-of-the-loan-charge (fetched 2026-07-06); launch news: https://www.gov.uk/government/news/loan-charge-review-launched (URL verified in search 2026-07-06).
- Outcome (search-result text quoting gov.uk/Practical Law, MEDIUM confidence on exact figures): report called the loan charge "extraordinary" and "undoubtedly harsh"; government accepted **all but one recommendation**, added a **£5,000 write-off** per individual, created a **new settlement opportunity** for ~**32,000** people with reductions "of up to £70,000". Corroborating: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/loan-charge-independent-review/loan-charge-review (fetched 2026-07-06 — confirms new review + response published) and https://www.litrg.org.uk/announcements/loan-charge-update.
- **Lever that worked:** a decade of backbench pressure (APPG letters, Finance Bill amendments, Westminster Hall debates) + affected-person evidence, converging on *commissioned independent reviews* — the strongest instrument short of litigation. Note the honest caveat: it took ~6 years and two reviews, and campaigners still dispute parts of the outcome.

### 1d. IR35 / off-payroll: the 2022 double U-turn

- **23 Sep 2022** (Growth Plan / "mini-budget", Kwarteng): government announced repeal of the 2017 and 2021 off-payroll working reforms from April 2023. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/the-growth-plan-2022-documents (fetched 2026-07-06; the repeal was in the Growth Plan document itself).
- **17 Oct 2022** (Hunt emergency statement): reversal, exact gov.uk wording: "Repealing the 2017 and 2021 reforms to the off-payroll working rules (also known as IR35) from April 2023. The reforms will now remain in place." Part of ~£32bn of reversals. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/news/chancellor-brings-forward-further-medium-term-fiscal-plan-measures (fetched 2026-07-06).
- **Honest reading:** contractor bodies (IPSE, Stop the Off-Payroll Tax campaign) lobbied for years and briefly "won" — but the win arrived only when it fitted a government's ideological programme, and evaporated in 3 weeks when gilt markets destroyed that programme. Lesson: **policy wins that ride a political wave die with the wave; wins built on administrative evidence (MTD delays, loan charge reviews) stick better.** (Attribution of the repeal to lobbying: LOW-MEDIUM confidence — the primary sources record only the decisions, not the cause.)

### 1e. Child Benefit HICBC threshold, Spring Budget 2024 — a media-led win

- **6 March 2024** (Spring Budget): HICBC threshold raised **£50,000 → £60,000** from 6 April 2024; taper halved so charge = 1% per £200 over £60,000, full clawback only at **£80,000**. Threshold had been frozen since 2013. Primary source (policy paper, published 6 Mar 2024): https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/income-tax-increasing-the-high-income-child-benefit-charge-threshold (fetched 2026-07-06).
- Campaign receipts: MoneySavingExpert article (6 Mar 2024) — Martin Lewis: "WE GOT THE WIN ON CHILD BENEFIT! Chancellor tipped me off before budget, said this was due in large to MSE/my shows campaigning all based on all those of you who messaged me to say it was the key thing to put to him." Campaign anatomy: mass reader messages → MSE evidence report on impact (single parents, carers) → open letter to the Chancellor (Jan 2024) → on-air interview pressure → Budget change. ~170,000 families taken out of the charge. Source: https://www.moneysavingexpert.com/news/2024/03/spring-budget-child-benefit-martin-lewis-mse/ (fetched 2026-07-06). Chancellor's crediting of MSE: as reported by Lewis — MEDIUM confidence (self-reported).
- Honest footnote: the same Budget promised consultation on moving HICBC to **household** income by April 2026 — that plan was later dropped by the next government (not verified today; do not publish without checking — flag LOW/unverified).
- Lesson: a trusted mass-media figure aggregating thousands of individual stories into one evidenced ask is one of the few ways "ordinary person" pressure visibly moves a Budget line. Individuals contributed by **feeding stories to the aggregator**.

## 3. Responding to consultations so it actually counts

- **Where to find them:** gov.uk open consultations filtered to HMRC: https://www.gov.uk/search/policy-papers-and-consultations?content_store_document_type%5B%5D=open_consultations&organisations%5B%5D=hm-revenue-customs (fetched 2026-07-06 — 14 HMRC consultations open today). Same filter works for HM Treasury.
- **What the rules say:** Cabinet Office **Consultation Principles** (2012, updated 19 Mar 2018): consult "only on issues that are genuinely undecided", use "clear language and plain English", make it "easier for the public to contribute their views". Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/consultation-principles-guidance (fetched 2026-07-06). The Tax Policy Making Principles (12 Jun 2025) add that formal tax consultation will be "targeted and precise" and run "a proportionate timeframe" — i.e. windows are getting shorter; watch openings.
- **What actually gets read:** evidence of impact beats opinion. Every winning case above (MTD Dec 2022, Small Business Review, loan charge reviews) turned on **quantified burden**: hours, £ cost, software cost, specific broken scenarios (jointly-owned property, no-NINO taxpayers — both became formal design changes on 22 Nov 2023). Structure a response as: who you are (one line), the specific proposal paragraph you're answering, your real numbers, what change you propose.
- **Format mechanics (live example, fetched 2026-07-06):** "Timely Payments in income tax Self Assessment" consultation — opened 23 Jun 2026, closes **11:59pm 4 Aug 2026**; respond by online form (forms.office.com link on page) or email **timelypayment@hmrc.gov.uk** or post: Helen Derbyshire, Timely Payment Team, HM Revenue and Customs, Trinity Bridge House, 2 Dearmans Place, 4th Floor, Manchester M3 5BS. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/timely-payments-in-income-tax-self-assessment. Every consultation page carries its own response route like this.
- **Where responses end up:** the same gov.uk consultation page later gains an "outcome" / summary-of-responses document (example: Small Business Review outcome page, 22 Nov 2023). Your words can literally appear (anonymised or attributed) in the summary.
- **Individuals ARE allowed to respond.** Nothing restricts consultations to bodies; a first-person sole-trader response with real numbers is rare and therefore memorable.

## 4. Budget representations — the direct written channel to HM Treasury

- Standing guidance ("Guidance for submitting your Budget representation", last updated 15 Jan 2021): "A Budget representation is a written representation from an **interest group, individual or representative body** to HM Treasury"; suggestions should "explain the policy rationale, costs, benefits and deliverability"; **"Representations will not receive a bespoke written reply."** Channels on the guidance page: online portal (per-event Smart Survey link), email **public.enquiries@hmtreasury.gov.uk**, post: The Correspondence and Enquiry Unit, HM Treasury, 1 Horse Guards Road, London, SW1A 2HQ. Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/budget-representations-guidance/guidance-for-submitting-your-budget-or-autumn-statement-representation (fetched 2026-07-06).
- Pattern: a fresh representations page + portal opens for each fiscal event once its date is announced (e.g. "Spring Budget 2024 representations" https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/spring-budget-2024-representations; Autumn Budget 2025 portal deadline was 15 Oct 2025 for the 26 Nov 2025 Budget — per Brevia, https://www.brevia.co.uk/news/politics/how-to-submit-a-representation-to-hm-treasury-for-autumn-budget-2025/, MEDIUM confidence). **As of 2026-07-06 no open representations portal for the next Budget was found on gov.uk** — tell readers to watch gov.uk once the next Budget date is announced.
- Honest rating: cheap to do, genuinely read as an input sweep before Budgets, zero feedback loop. Best used for a **specific, costed, deliverable ask** (e.g. "raise the MTD exemption application processing standard"), not general complaint.

- Being in the system (even voluntarily) + reporting real friction through Tell ABAB / LITRG / your software vendor's HMRC channel is currently the most direct feedback loop an ordinary MTD user has.

## 7. Realistic expectations — what moves the needle

- **One personal letter to your MP:** gets a logged reply; department must answer the MP within a 20-working-day standard (Cabinet Office correspondence data, §5). Changes policy alone: almost never. Builds the casework pile that MPs cite in debates: yes.
- **100 identical letters:** actively discounted. WriteToThem (which sends the letters!) blocks "identikit" messages because "MPs rather naturally take a sudden influx of identical or similar messages with a large pinch of salt" — they aim to be "a trusted source of genuine letters from genuine constituents". Source: https://www.writetothem.com/about-qa (fetched 2026-07-06). Petitions are the honest venue for raw numbers, and even 100,000 signatures buys a debate, not a decision (petition.parliament.uk/help).
- **One well-evidenced submission:** the format every receipt in §1 traces back to. The 2017 MTD deferral credited committee + professional-body **evidence**; the Dec 2022 delay followed costed unreadiness evidence; loan charge concessions came from reviews weighing submitted case evidence; HICBC moved when MSE converted thousands of anecdotes into one evidence report + one open letter. Quality, aggregated, beats quantity, duplicated.
- **Timescales, honestly:** MTD delays took 1–2 years per cycle of report→announcement; the loan charge took ~6 years and two reviews for partial redress; HICBC took ~2 years of concerted media campaigning against a 11-year-frozen threshold. And wins reverse (IR35 in 3 weeks; MTD threshold back down to £20k in 2025). Tell readers: aim your effort at consultations, Tell ABAB, LITRG evidence calls, and your MP — with numbers, not adjectives — and treat petitions/social media as awareness tools, not levers.
