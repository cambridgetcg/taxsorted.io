## 1. The two-department split: Treasury decides, HMRC runs it (the "policy partnership")

**HM Treasury** is "the government's economic and finance ministry, maintaining control over public spending, setting the direction of the UK's economic policy and working to achieve strong and sustainable economic growth."
Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-treasury (fetched 2026-07-06)

**HMRC** is "a non-ministerial Department established by the Commissioners for Revenue and Customs Act (CRCA) 2005" and "report[s] to Parliament through our Treasury minister who oversees our spending."
Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/about (fetched 2026-07-06)

The division of tax-policy labour is stated on HMRC's own gov.uk About page, verbatim:

> "The Treasury lead on strategic tax policy and policy development. HMRC leads on policy maintenance and implementation. This arrangement for policy making is known as the 'policy partnership'."

Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/about (fetched 2026-07-06)

Plain-English version for the site: **Treasury ministers decide what the tax rules should be (rates, new taxes, reliefs — announced at Budgets). HMRC keeps the machine running (collects the tax, runs the systems, maintains existing rules).** If you want a rule *changed*, the Treasury is the decision-maker; if you have a problem with how an existing rule is *applied to you*, that's HMRC.

Historical origin note: this split was formalised after the 2010 "Tax policy making: a new approach" reform — the old gov.uk publication URL (`/government/publications/tax-policy-making-a-new-approach`) now 404s, so cite the live HMRC About page above instead. (Origin claim: confidence LOW — not re-verified from a primary source today.)

Governance link between the two: the **Exchequer Secretary to the Treasury chairs the HMRC Board** (per HMRC's governance page).
Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/about/our-governance (fetched 2026-07-06)

## 4. Why writing via your own MP gets a minister's reply (the correspondence convention)

Primary source: Cabinet Office, **"Guide to Handling Correspondence" (September 2024)**.
Publication page: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/handling-government-correspondence-guidance (updated 6 September 2024)
PDF: https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/66d99762608fb761b681111d/Guide_to_Handling_Correspondence_-_September_2024__1_.pdf (read in full 2026-07-06)

Exact quotes (paragraph numbers from the PDF):

- Para 7: **"Ministerial correspondence consists of any correspondence that requires a response from a minister, including letters and emails from Members of Parliament (MPs), Members of the House of Lords (Peers), and Members of the Devolved Parliaments or Assemblies."**
- Para 8: **"Public (or 'Treat Official') correspondence consists of letters or emails from members of the public or organisations and is replied to by officials rather than ministers."**
- Para 9: departmental reply deadlines "should not exceed **20 working days**. The clock (i.e. the first working day) starts the day after a piece of correspondence is received."
- Para 34: **"All correspondence to ministers from MPs, Peers and Members of the Devolved Parliaments and Assemblies should be signed off at an appropriate ministerial level."**
- Para 35: official (non-ministerial) replies to MP letters should only happen for day-to-day agency operations or where a minister decides it's more appropriate.
- Para 4: "The default method for MPs, Peers … and members of the public to correspond with Government departments is via official departmental email addresses, rather than by hard copy."
- Para 15: where an MP writes with a constituent's details, the response goes to the MP's office and quotes the constituent's full address.

Plain-English version for the site: **write to the Treasury yourself and an official answers; ask your MP to write for you and a minister has to sign the reply.** That's the whole trick — it's a published Cabinet Office rule, not folklore. Target turnaround either way: 20 working days.

### Finding and using your MP
- **WriteToThem** (mySociety charity, free): "Write to your politicians, national or local, for free" — enter your postcode, pick your MP, send. Source: https://www.writetothem.com/ (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **TheyWorkForYou** (mySociety, free): look up representatives by postcode; debates (Hansard), voting records, email alerts. Source: https://www.theyworkforyou.com/ (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Official directory: members.parliament.uk "Find Your MP" — URL https://members.parliament.uk/FindYourMP (confidence LOW: parliament.uk returned HTTP 403 to our fetcher on 2026-07-06, so not verified today; verify before publishing)

### Petitions (a separate, weaker lever — for completeness)
- "Petitions that get 10,000 signatures get a response from the UK Government."
- "All petitions that get 100,000 signatures will be considered for debate, and are usually debated."
- British citizens and UK residents can create and sign.
Source: https://petition.parliament.uk/help (fetched 2026-07-06)

## 5. Budget representations — the formal "tell the Chancellor" channel

**What it is** (standing HMT guidance, exact quote):
> "a written representation from an interest group, individual or representative body to HM Treasury with the aim of commenting on government policy and suggesting new policy ideas."

**Who can submit:** an interest group, **individual** or representative body — i.e. any citizen, sole trader or landlord, not just lobbyists.

**How:** via HMT's online portal for each fiscal event (preferred), or by post to The Correspondence and Enquiry Unit, HM Treasury, 1 Horse Guards Road, London SW1A 2HQ.

**What it should contain:** "policy suggestions for the upcoming fiscal event and explain the policy rationale, costs, benefits and deliverability" — the guidance suggests covering effectiveness, revenue implications, macroeconomic/sectoral/distributional effects, environmental impact.

Source (standing guidance, published 15 January 2021, still the live guidance page): https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/budget-representations-guidance/guidance-for-submitting-your-budget-or-autumn-statement-representation (fetched 2026-07-06)
Parent page: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/budget-representations-guidance

**Deadline pattern — verified against the most recent completed round:**
- 3 September 2025: HMT announced Budget date (Wednesday 26 November 2025) and simultaneously opened the representations portal; "The portal will close at 23:59 on Wednesday 15 October"; "The representations will be collated and shared with HMT policy officials."
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/news/budget-to-address-economy-thats-not-working-well-enough-for-working-people (published 3 September 2025; fetched 2026-07-06)
- Pattern: **portal opens when the Budget date is announced (roughly 2–3 months out) and closes about six weeks before Budget day.** Each fiscal event gets its own gov.uk page/portal (e.g. "Spring Budget 2024 representations": https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/spring-budget-2024-representations).

## 6. Who's political, who's neutral: special advisers vs civil servants

**Civil servants** (the permanent machine — includes the Treasury officials and HMRC staff who actually draft tax policy and run the system). The Civil Service Code's four values, exact quotes:
- integrity — "putting the obligations of public service above your own personal interests"
- honesty — "being truthful and open"
- objectivity — "basing your advice and decisions on rigorous analysis of the evidence"
- impartiality — "acting solely according to the merits of the case and serving equally well governments of different political persuasions"
They must not "act in a way that is determined by party political considerations, or use official resources for party political purposes."
Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/civil-service-code/the-civil-service-code (fetched 2026-07-06)

**Special advisers ("spads")** — the political layer around ministers. From the Code of Conduct for Special Advisers (September 2024 edition, updated 6 November 2024), exact quotes/points:
- They "add a political dimension to the advice and assistance available to ministers while reinforcing the political impartiality of the permanent Civil Service."
- They are **temporary civil servants** appointed under Part 1 of the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010, exempt from the requirements to be "appointed on merit and behave with impartiality and objectivity".
- "All appointments of special advisers require the prior written approval of the Prime Minister." Appointments end "at the end of the Administration which appointed them or when the appointing minister leaves the Government or moves to another appointment."
- They must not "authorise expenditure of public funds or have responsibility for budgets" and may not manage permanent civil servants.
Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/special-advisers-code-of-conduct/code-of-conduct-for-special-advisers-html (fetched 2026-07-06)
Publication page: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/special-advisers-code-of-conduct

Plain-English version: **civil servants are permanent and party-neutral; spads are the Chancellor's political appointees and leave when their minister does. Neither can be lobbied through official channels the way ministers can — your letters land with the correspondence unit (officials) or, via your MP, with a minister.**

## 7. The OBR — independent forecasts, not policy

- gov.uk: "The Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) gives independent and authoritative analysis of the UK's public finances." Status: "executive non-departmental public body, sponsored by HM Treasury". Website: https://obr.uk/
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/office-for-budget-responsibility (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Statutory basis — Budget Responsibility and National Audit Act 2011:
  - s4(1): **"It is the duty of the Office to examine and report on the sustainability of the public finances."**
  - s4(3)(a): it must prepare fiscal and economic forecasts "on at least two occasions for each financial year" (these are the forecasts published alongside Budgets and other fiscal events).
  - s5: the Office "has complete discretion in the performance of its duty", "must perform that duty objectively, transparently and impartially", and — the key line for our audience — where government policies are relevant it "must have regard to those policies, but **may not consider what the effect of any alternative policies would be**."
  Sources: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2011/4/section/4 and https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2011/4/section/5 (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Fetch note: https://obr.uk/about-the-obr/what-we-do/ returned HTTP 403 to our fetcher on 2026-07-06; the gov.uk + legislation.gov.uk sources above are sufficient primary anchors.

Plain-English version: **the OBR marks the government's homework (are the numbers credible?) but never sets the questions. It can't propose or veto a tax change, and by law it isn't allowed to model the opposition's alternative plans.**
