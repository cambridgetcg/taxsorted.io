## 1. What HMRC is

### 1.1 Department type — and why it matters

- gov.uk states: **"HMRC is a non-ministerial department, supported by 1 public body."**
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Purpose (exact wording): *"We collect the money that pays for the UK's public services, help
  families and individuals with targeted financial support and provide the property valuations
  that support taxation and benefits."* (same source)
- HMRC's About page: HMRC is *"a non-ministerial Department established by the Commissioners for
  Revenue and Customs Act (CRCA) 2005"*, with responsibility vested *"in Commissioners appointed
  by the King"* drawn from senior management.
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/about (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Relationship with ministers: HMRC *"report[s] to Parliament through our Treasury minister who
  oversees our spending."* Treasury and HMRC work in a **"policy partnership"**: Treasury leads on
  strategic tax policy; HMRC handles implementation/administration. (same About source)
- **Why "non-ministerial" matters for ordinary taxpayers (plain-words framing for content):**
  day-to-day administration of the tax system legally rests with HMRC's Commissioners, not with
  politicians. A minister or MP cannot simply order HMRC to change the outcome of *your*
  individual case. That is deliberate — it protects impartiality (About page: HMRC administers the
  system *"by being impartial and increasingly effective and efficient"*). The routes that DO work
  for an individual case are: statutory appeal/review, the complaints ladder (section 4), the
  Adjudicator, and the Ombudsman via your MP. MPs *can* however chase HMRC on your behalf and
  raise systemic issues — that's the policy lever, covered in the wider pillar.
  (Interpretive paragraph — anchored to CRCA/About quotes above; the "ministers don't direct
  individual cases" sentence is our plain-words gloss, not a direct quote.)
- HMRC's five strategic objectives (About page, fetched 2026-07-06): close the tax gap; improve
  day-to-day performance and customer experience; reform and modernise tax administration; build a
  high-performing workforce; support wider government economic goals.
- Key responsibility quote: HMRC is *"responsible for safeguarding the flow of money to the
  Exchequer through our collection, compliance and enforcement activities."* (About page)

## 2. The HMRC Charter — your rights, their obligations

Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-charter/the-hmrc-charter
(page marked "Updated: 30 July 2024"; fetched 2026-07-06)

- **Legal basis:** the Charter is required by the **Commissioners for Revenue and Customs Act
  2005** — section 16A. Statutory text (verified at
  https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/11/section/16A, fetched 2026-07-06):
  - *"The Commissioners must prepare a Charter"* and must *"regularly review the Charter, and
    publish revisions, or revised versions, of it when they consider it appropriate to do so."*
  - The Charter *"must include standards of behaviour and values to which Her Majesty's Revenue
    and Customs will aspire when dealing with people in the exercise of their functions."*
  - **Annual accountability:** *"The Commissioners must, at least once every year, make a report
    reviewing the extent to which Her Majesty's Revenue and Customs have demonstrated the
    standards of behaviour and values included in the Charter."*
- **The seven Charter standards** (headings exact):
  1. "Getting things right"
  2. "Making things easy"
  3. "Being responsive"
  4. "Treating you fairly"
  5. "Being aware of your personal situation"
  6. "Recognising that someone can represent you"
  7. "Keeping your data secure"
- **What HMRC expects back:** *"full, accurate and timely answers"* when it asks for information;
  it *"takes threats, intimidation or harassment very seriously"* and expects professional
  treatment of staff.
- **Where it binds (plain words):** it is a statutory document ("aspire" wording = standards, not
  hard rights you can sue on), but it has teeth in three places: (a) HMRC must report annually on
  Charter performance (s16A); (b) you can quote it in a complaint (tier 1/tier 2); (c) the
  Adjudicator's Office tests whether HMRC *"applied its rules, standards, guidance and codes of
  practice fairly and consistently"* — the Charter is part of that yardstick.
  (Adjudicator quote: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/the-adjudicator-s-office, fetched 2026-07-06.)

## 4. The complaints ladder (tier 1 → tier 2 → Adjudicator → Ombudsman)

Primary source: https://www.gov.uk/complain-about-hmrc (fetched 2026-07-06)

**Step 0 — is it a complaint?** Complaints ≠ appeals. If you think a tax decision or penalty is
*wrong*, use the appeal/review route instead. Complaints are for poor service: *"unreasonable
delays, mistakes and poor treatment."* Keep paying tax during a complaint to avoid interest and
penalties.

**Tier 1 — first review by HMRC.** Complain online (individual/business/agent forms, sign-in
needed), by phone, or by post. Have your NI number, UTR or VAT number ready, plus what happened
and how you want it resolved. HMRC *"investigate what happened and what should have happened."*

**Tier 2 — second review by HMRC.** A different person reviews how the first complaint was
handled. *"The decision from the second tier review is final"* (within HMRC).

**Tier 3 — Adjudicator's Office.** *"This service is free and independent of HMRC."*
- What it is: *"The Adjudicator's Office investigates complaints about HM Revenue and Customs
  (HMRC) and the Valuation Office Agency (VOA)"*; it checks whether HMRC *"applied its rules,
  standards, guidance and codes of practice fairly and consistently."*
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/the-adjudicator-s-office (fetched 2026-07-06)
- The Adjudicator (as of 2026-07-06): **Mike McMahon**. (same source)
- Precondition: you must have completed HMRC's first review and (if you disagreed) second review
  first. Source: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/contact-the-adjudicators-office (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Contact (exact, fetched 2026-07-06):**
  - Phone: `0300 057 1111` — "Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm. Closed weekends and bank holidays."
  - Post: `The Adjudicator's Office, PO Box 11222, Nottingham, NG2 9AD`
  - Online form: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/contact-the-adjudicators-office-online
    (plus a printable form: "Ask the Adjudicator's Office to investigate a complaint or review a decision")
  - No public email published on the contact page.

**Tier 4 — Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (PHSO), via an MP.**
- gov.uk: after the Adjudicator you can go to the Parliamentary Ombudsman — *only by asking your
  MP to refer the complaint*. Source: https://www.gov.uk/complain-about-hmrc (fetched 2026-07-06)
- PHSO's own wording (fetched 2026-07-06,
  https://www.ombudsman.org.uk/making-complaint/information-mps/helping-your-constituents-use-our-service):
  *"by law, complaints about UK Government departments and other UK public organisations must be
  referred to us by an MP. This is known as the 'MP filter'."* And: *"We can only look at cases
  about UK Government departments if an MP refers them to us."*
- The MP filter comes from the Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1967 (PHSO: the Parliamentary
  Ombudsman *"draws her powers almost wholly from the Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1967, as
  since amended"* — https://www.ombudsman.org.uk/about-us/who-we-are/legislation/legislation-parliamentary-ombudsman, fetched 2026-07-06).
- PHSO helpline: `0345 015 4033` — Mon–Thu 9am–4pm, Fri 8:30am–12pm; complaint checker tool:
  https://complaintform.ombudsman.org.uk/complaintchecker. Stated wait for UK government
  department cases: "up to seven months".
  Source: https://www.ombudsman.org.uk/making-complaint (fetched 2026-07-06)
- (Civic tie-in: find your MP via writetothem.com or parliament.uk — covered in the pillar's MP page.)

**Money back:** HMRC may refund *"reasonable costs directly caused by their mistakes or delays"*
— postage, phone charges, professional fees; keep receipts. Source: gov.uk/complain-about-hmrc.

**Complaints post addresses (exact, fetched 2026-07-06,
https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/contact/complain-about-hmrc):**

## 5. Accountability artifacts — where to check HMRC's homework

- **Annual report and accounts** — published each summer; latest: **"HMRC annual report and
  accounts: 2024 to 2025", published 17 July 2025**. Reports *"outline how HMRC has performed"*
  and *"analyse HMRC's past performance, and set out future challenges."* Includes the statutory
  Charter performance review (CRCA s16A).
  Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/hmrcs-annual-report-and-accounts (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Tax gap** — annual, every June: "Measuring tax gaps". Latest: **2026 edition, published
  23 June 2026**, estimates for 2024-25: *"6.4% of total theoretical tax liabilities, or £59.2
  billion in absolute terms, which means HMRC collected 93.6% of all tax due."* Breakdown by
  customer group 2024-25: **small businesses 62%** (up from 58% in 2020-21), large businesses 12%,
  criminals 8%, mid-sized businesses 7%, wealthy 6%, individuals 4%. (Directly relevant to
  TaxSorted's audience: HMRC's own numbers say small business error/evasion is the biggest slice —
  which drives MTD and compliance policy aimed at sole traders/landlords.)
  Sources: https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/measuring-tax-gaps and
  https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/measuring-tax-gaps/1-tax-gaps-summary (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Customer service performance** — two artifacts:
  - Historical: "HMRC monthly performance reports" — *"monthly summaries of how HMRC has performed
    against its customer service targets"* covering digital services use, "answering our phones",
    "handling customer post", "responding to complaints". Last separate monthly report: March 2025
    (published 17 July 2025). From tax year 2025-26 they are folded into the performance updates.
    Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/hmrc-monthly-performance-reports (fetched 2026-07-06)
  - Current: **"HMRC performance updates"** collection (monthly + quarterly data in one) — tracks
    the strategic objectives incl. "close the tax gap" and "improve day-to-day performance and the
    overall customer experience". Latest seen: **"HMRC performance update: February 2026",
    published 9 April 2026** (roughly a two-month lag; re-check before publishing).
    Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/hmrc-quarterly-performance-updates (fetched 2026-07-06)
- Also relevant (from governance page): the Board has standing sub-committees for **Customer
  Service** and **Closing the Tax Gap** — the internal mirror of these publications.

## 6. How HMRC works with software developers — and where TaxSorted fits

- **HMRC Developer Hub** — https://developer.service.hmrc.gov.uk/ — *"Create tax software and apps
  using HMRC APIs."* Developers can: get an account, explore API documentation
  (/api-documentation/docs/api), test in the **sandbox** (incl. "Create a test user"), and manage
  registered applications. Both REST and legacy XML APIs. (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Developer support portal** — https://developer.service.hmrc.gov.uk/developer/support — tiered
  form (finding an API / using an API / account access / application management / other);
  commitment: *"The team will reply within 2 UK business days."* (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Software Developers Support Team (SDST)** — gov.uk contact:
  https://www.gov.uk/find-hmrc-contacts/software-developers-enquiries — email
  **`SDSTeam@hmrc.gov.uk`** — helps with *"technical documentation, for example schema and
  validation rules"*, *"vendor IDs and testing credentials"*, *"giving feedback or advice during
  testing"*, and **"product recognition"**. (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **MTD ITSA end-to-end service guide** —
  https://developer.service.hmrc.gov.uk/guides/income-tax-mtd-end-to-end-service-guide/ — *"This
  service guide explains how you can integrate your software with the Making Tax Digital for
  Income Tax APIs."* Covers minimum functionality standards, production access approval, quarterly
  updates, final declarations; changelog on GitHub (`income-tax-mtd-changelog`). (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **The public software list** —
  https://www.gov.uk/guidance/find-software-thats-compatible-with-making-tax-digital-for-income-tax —
  *"All software listed has been through HMRC's recognition process. HMRC does not recommend any
  product or software provider."* Free options acknowledged: *"free products are available for
  those with simple tax affairs but there may be limits on how the product can be used"*. Users
  find products via HMRC's "software finder tool". (fetched 2026-07-06)
- **Where TaxSorted fits (working notes, not sourced facts):**
  1. Register on the Developer Hub → sandbox → production access under the MTD ITSA minimum
     functionality standards.
  2. Use SDSTeam@hmrc.gov.uk for schema/validation questions and to enter **product recognition**
     so TaxSorted appears in the official software finder — as a *free* option, directly serving
     the audience HMRC's own tax-gap stats say most needs help (small businesses = 62% of the gap).
  3. The developer hub is also a consultation channel: API roadmap/changelog feedback and user
     research surveys are how developers influence MTD design between formal consultations.
