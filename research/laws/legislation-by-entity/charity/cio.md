## Key Principle

> A CIO is a legal form created **specifically for charities**. Unlike charitable companies, CIOs are regulated **only by the Charity Commission** - NOT Companies House.

**Advantages over Charitable Company**:
- Single regulator (Charity Commission only)
- Single set of accounts
- Simpler constitutional requirements
- Still has legal personality and limited liability

## Charities Act 2011 Part 11: CIOs

### Section 204: Definition

```
A charitable incorporated organisation ("CIO") is a body
corporate—
(a) which is constituted in accordance with this Part of this
    Act, and
(b) whose purposes are exclusively charitable.
```

### Section 205: Constitution

```
(1) A CIO must have a constitution.
(2) The constitution must state—
    (a) the name of the CIO,
    (b) the purposes of the CIO,
    (c) whether the principal office is in England or Wales.
```

### Section 206: Members and Trustees

Two types of CIO:

| Type | Structure |
|------|-----------|
| **Foundation CIO** | Trustees are the only voting members |
| **Association CIO** | Wider membership (like a company) |

### Section 207: Limited Liability

```
The members of a CIO do not (as such) become liable to contribute
to the assets of the CIO if it is wound up.
```

**Protection**: Like a company limited by guarantee, members' liability is limited.

### Section 214: Registration

```
A CIO is registered when the Commission enter it in the
register of charities.
```

**Contrast with company**: No Companies House registration needed.

## Tax Treatment

### Identical to Charitable Companies

CIOs receive the same tax exemptions under CTA 2010 Part 11:

| Exemption | Section | Scope |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Trading** | s.478-479 | Primary purpose + small trading |
| **Property** | s.486 | Rental income |
| **Investment** | s.492 | Dividends, interest |
| **Gains** | TCGA s.256 | Capital gains |

### Why Same Treatment?

CTA 2010 s.467 defines "charity" broadly:
```
"charity" means a body of persons or trust which—
(a) is established for charitable purposes only, and
(b) meets the jurisdictional condition.
```

CIOs meet this definition, so same exemptions apply.

## Conversion to/from CIO

### From Charitable Company to CIO

Charities Act 2011 s.228-234:

```
CONVERSION PROCESS

1. Charity trustees resolve to convert
2. Apply to Charity Commission
3. CC registers CIO, dissolves company
4. Assets and liabilities transfer automatically
```

**Advantage**: Single step, no need for asset transfer agreements.

### From Unincorporated Charity to CIO

Charities Act 2011 s.235-243:
- More complex (unincorporated has no legal personality)
- Assets must be transferred to new CIO
- Trustees execute deed of transfer

## CIO vs Charitable Company Comparison

| Aspect | CIO | Charitable Company |
|--------|-----|-------------------|
| **Legal Personality** | Yes | Yes |
| **Limited Liability** | Yes | Yes |
| **Regulators** | CC only | CC + Companies House |
| **Accounts Filing** | CC only | CC + Companies House |
| **Constitution** | CIO constitution | Memorandum + Articles |
| **Conversion** | Direct | To CIO possible |
| **Age** | From 2013 | Long established |

---

## Advantages of CIO

1. **Single regulator**: Only Charity Commission
2. **Single accounts**: One filing (not two)
3. **Simpler governance**: Charity-specific constitution
4. **Modern framework**: Designed for charities

## Disadvantages of CIO

1. **Less familiar**: Newer form (2013)
2. **Less case law**: Untested in some areas
3. **No Companies House search**: Creditors may be unfamiliar
4. **Some lenders unfamiliar**: May prefer company
