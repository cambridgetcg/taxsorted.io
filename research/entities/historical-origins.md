## 1. INDIVIDUALS (Natural Persons)

### Origin of Individual Taxation

Individual taxation in the UK traces back to **1799** when Prime Minister **William Pitt the Younger** introduced the first income tax to fund the Napoleonic Wars.

#### The First Income Tax (1799)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Date** | 9 January 1799 |
| **Legislation** | Introduced by William Pitt the Younger |
| **Purpose** | Fund wars against Napoleon's France |
| **Initial Rate** | Progressive: 2d in the pound (1/120th) on incomes >£60, up to 2 shillings (10%) on incomes >£200 |
| **Expected Revenue** | £10 million (actual: £6 million) |

#### Addington's 1803 Act - The Modern Foundation

Henry Addington reintroduced income tax in 1803 with two revolutionary innovations:

1. **Taxation at Source** - Deduction before payment (e.g., Bank of England deducting from gilt interest)
2. **The Five Schedules System**:

| Schedule | Covered |
|----------|---------|
| **A** | Income from land and buildings |
| **B** | Farming profits |
| **C** | Public annuities |
| **D** | Self-employment and other income |
| **E** | Salaries, annuities and pensions |

> *"It was Henry Addington's Act of 1803 that brought in schedules and cases, together with deduction at source."* - ATT

#### 1816 Abolition and 1842 Permanent Return

- **1816**: Income tax abolished "with a thundering peal of applause" after Waterloo
- Parliament ordered destruction of all tax documents (though duplicates were retained)
- **1842**: Sir Robert Peel reintroduced income tax at 7d in the pound on incomes >£150
- Though promoted as "temporary," it has been levied continuously since

### Key Amendments - Individual Taxation

| Year | Amendment | Change |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1799 | Original Income Tax Act | First introduction |
| 1803 | Addington's Act | Schedules A-E, deduction at source |
| 1842 | Income Tax Act 1842 | Permanent reintroduction |
| 1965 | Finance Act 1965 | Capital Gains Tax introduced (30%) |
| 1973 | Finance Act 1972 | VAT introduced |
| 1996 | Finance Act 1994/1996 | Self Assessment introduced |
| 2007 | Income Tax Act 2007 | Modern consolidation |

## 2. SOLE TRADERS (Self-Employed Individuals)

### Origin

Sole traders are not a separate legal entity but individuals trading in business. Their tax treatment evolved through **Schedule D** of Addington's 1803 Act.

#### Schedule D History

| Period | Treatment |
|--------|-----------|
| 1803-2005 | Schedule D Case I (Trade) and Case II (Professions) |
| 2005 onwards | Trading income (Schedule terminology abolished) |
| 2012 | HMRC stopped using "Schedule D" terminology |

#### Modern Self-Employment Structure

The term "sole trader" refers to:
- An individual carrying on business alone
- Not a separate legal person from the owner
- Taxed via Self Assessment since 1996-97
- Subject to Class 2 and Class 4 National Insurance

### Key Amendments - Sole Trader Taxation

| Year | Amendment | Change |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1803 | Addington's Act | Schedule D for trading income |
| 1911 | National Insurance Act | NI contributions for workers |
| 1975 | Social Security Act | Class 2 & 4 NI for self-employed |
| 1996 | Finance Act 1994 | Self Assessment introduced |
| 2017 | Finance Act 2017 | Class 2 NI reforms announced |
| 2024+ | MTD for ITSA | Making Tax Digital requirements |

## 3. PARTNERSHIPS

### Origin

Partnership law in England developed through **common law** before being codified in the **Partnership Act 1890**.

#### Pre-1890 Development

- Common law governed partnerships for centuries
- 1865: *Cox v Hickman* (House of Lords) established that partnership arises from "carrying on business in common with a view to profit"
- This definition was incorporated into the 1890 Act

#### Partnership Act 1890 (53 & 54 Vict. c. 39)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 14 August 1890 |
| **Definition** | "The relation which subsists between persons carrying on a business in common with a view of profit" |
| **Key Feature** | Unlimited liability for all partners |
| **Max Partners** | Originally 20, unlimited since 2002 |

#### Limited Partnerships Act 1907

Created a hybrid structure with:
- At least one **general partner** (unlimited liability, management rights)
- One or more **limited partners** (liability limited to contribution, no management)

### Key Amendments - Partnership Law

| Year | Amendment | Change |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1890 | Partnership Act 1890 | Codified partnership law |
| 1907 | Limited Partnerships Act | Created limited partnerships |
| 2000 | LLP Act | Created LLPs (see below) |
| 2002 | Regulatory Reform Order | Removed 20-partner limit |

### Tax Treatment

Partnerships are "tax transparent":
- Partnership itself does not pay tax
- Each partner taxed on their share of profits
- Partnership files SA800 return
- Partners file individual Self Assessment

## 4. LIMITED COMPANIES

### Origin

The modern limited company emerged through a series of Victorian-era statutes that revolutionised business organisation.

#### Pre-1844: Royal Charter and Private Acts

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Royal Charter | Granted by the Crown (e.g., East India Company, 1600) |
| Private Act of Parliament | Expensive, ~100 companies incorporated 1801-1844 |
| Letters Patent (from 1834) | Crown grant for specific purposes |

#### Joint Stock Companies Act 1844

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 1844 |
| **Champion** | William Gladstone (Parliamentary Committee chair, 1843) |
| **Innovation** | Incorporation by **registration** rather than state permission |
| **Created** | Office of Registrar of Joint Stock Companies (precursor to Companies House) |
| **Limitation** | No limited liability - members still personally liable |

> *"For the first time in at least 500 years corporations could be formed without explicit, deliberate, and specific State permission."* - Harris (2000)

#### Limited Liability Act 1855

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 1855 |
| **Innovation** | First allowed **limited liability** for shareholders |
| **Effect** | Shareholders only liable for unpaid amount on shares |
| **Significance** | Removed personal liability barrier to investment |

#### Companies Act 1862

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 1862 |
| **Innovation** | Comprehensive framework; Memorandum of Association |
| **Company Types** | Limited by shares, limited by guarantee |
| **Legacy** | Model for company law throughout British Empire |

> *"The United Kingdom was the first country to draft modern corporation statutes."*

### Key Amendments - Company Law

| Year | Amendment | Change |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1844 | Joint Stock Companies Act | Registration-based incorporation |
| 1855 | Limited Liability Act | Shareholder liability limited |
| 1862 | Companies Act | Consolidated framework |
| 1907 | Companies Act | Private companies distinguished |
| 1948 | Companies Act | Post-war modernisation |
| 1985 | Companies Act | Major consolidation |
| 2006 | Companies Act | Current framework (1,300 sections) |

### Corporation Tax History

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| Pre-1965 | Companies paid income tax + profits tax |
| 1965 | **Finance Act 1965** introduced Corporation Tax at 40% |
| 1969 | Rate increased to 45% |
| 1973 | Rate 52% + small companies' rate 42%; ACT introduced |
| 1984 | Rate reduced to 35% |
| 1999 | ACT abolished |
| 2015 | Rate reduced to 20% |
| 2017 | Rate reduced to 19% |
| 2023 | Rate increased to 25% (profits >£250k) |

## 5. LIMITED LIABILITY PARTNERSHIPS (LLPs)

### Origin

LLPs are a relatively modern creation, driven by professional services firms seeking liability protection.

#### US Origins and UK Adoption

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| Late 1980s | US Savings & Loans crisis - accountancy/law firms face massive claims |
| 1991 | **Texas** introduces first LLP legislation |
| 1996 | **Jersey** passes LLP legislation; Big Six accountancy firms lobby UK |
| 1999 | Bill introduced to House of Lords (November) |
| 2000 | **Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000** receives Royal Assent (20 July) |
| 2001 | Act comes into force **6 April 2001** (aligned with tax year) |
| 2003 | All Big Four auditing firms converted to LLP |

#### Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2000 (c. 12)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Combine partnership flexibility with corporate liability protection |
| **Legal Status** | Separate legal personality (unlike traditional partnership) |
| **Governance** | Hybrid of company and partnership law |
| **Tax Treatment** | Tax transparent (like partnership) - members taxed individually |
| **Lobbying** | Driven by Big Four accountancy firms |

### Key Regulations

| Year | Regulation | Purpose |
|------|------------|---------|
| 2001 | LLP Regulations 2001 | Detailed operating rules |
| 2009 | LLP (Application of Companies Act 2006) Regulations | Applied CA 2006 provisions |

## 6. CHARITIES

### Origin

English charity law has medieval roots, codified during Elizabeth I's reign.

#### Pre-1601: Medieval Charity

- Charitable work primarily through the Church
- **1538**: Henry VIII dissolved monasteries, confiscating Church assets
- Resulting poverty crisis led to statutory intervention

#### Charitable Uses Act 1601 (43 Eliz. 1. c. 4)

Also known as the **Statute of Elizabeth** or **Statute of Charitable Uses**.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 1601 |
| **Context** | Part of Elizabethan Poor Laws addressing poverty crisis |
| **Purpose** | "To redress the Misemployment of Lands Goods and Stocks of Money heretofore given to Charitable Uses" |
| **Key Feature** | Preamble contained first recorded list of charitable purposes |

#### The Preamble's Lasting Impact

The 1601 Preamble listed charitable purposes including:
- Relief of aged, impotent, and poor people
- Maintenance of sick and maimed soldiers and mariners
- Schools of learning, free schools, scholars in universities
- Repair of bridges, ports, havens, causeways, churches
- Education and preferment of orphans
- Relief of prisoners
- Marriage of poor maids

> *"Courts have tended to look for an analogy between a purpose under consideration and the 1601 list."*

#### Evolution of Charity Law

| Year | Legislation | Change |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1601 | Charitable Uses Act | First codification |
| 1888 | Mortmain and Charitable Uses Act | Repealed 1601 Act but preserved preamble |
| 1960 | Charities Act | Modern regulatory framework; repealed 1888 Act |
| 1992 | Charities Act | Enhanced regulation |
| 1993 | Charities Act | Consolidation |
| 2006 | Charities Act | New definition; Charity Tribunal; registration threshold £5,000 |
| 2011 | Charities Act | Current consolidation |
| 2022 | Charities Act | Further reforms |

#### Modern Definition (Charities Act 2006/2011)

Charity must be established for **charitable purposes** and provide **public benefit**:
1. Prevention/relief of poverty
2. Advancement of education
3. Advancement of religion
4. Advancement of health
5. Advancement of citizenship/community development
6. Advancement of arts, culture, heritage, science
7. Advancement of amateur sport
8. Advancement of human rights, conflict resolution, equality
9. Advancement of environmental protection
10. Relief of those in need
11. Advancement of animal welfare
12. Promotion of efficiency of armed forces/emergency services
13. Other purposes beneficial to community

### Tax Treatment

Charities enjoy significant tax reliefs:
- Exempt from Income Tax and Corporation Tax on charitable activities
- Gift Aid allows recovery of basic rate tax on donations
- Exempt from Capital Gains Tax
- 80%+ relief on business rates
- Stamp Duty Land Tax relief

## 7. TRUSTS

### Origin

Trust law evolved from medieval "uses" - a uniquely English legal innovation.

#### Medieval Origins: The "Use"

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| 12th-13th C | "Uses" developed, possibly for Franciscan friars (forbidden to own property) |
| Crusades | Knights conveyed land to trustees ("feoffees") to manage during absence |
| 14th-15th C | Uses proliferated to avoid feudal obligations and inheritance taxes |

> *"When knights went to war and left their property at the disposal of other persons, it was necessary to implement the will of the initial owner whilst enabling the empowered person to manage this property effectively."*

#### Statute of Uses 1535/1536

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 1535 (effective 1536) |
| **Sponsor** | Henry VIII |
| **Purpose** | Abolish uses to restore feudal tax revenue |
| **Method** | "Executed" uses - made beneficiary the legal owner |
| **Outcome** | Led to development of the **trust** as a workaround |

> *"The statute invalidated all uses that did not impose an active duty on trustees, with beneficiaries of the use being held as the legal owners of the land, meaning they had to pay tax."*

#### Evolution into Modern Trust

Lawyers circumvented the Statute of Uses by:
- Creating "use upon a use" (the second use not executed)
- Imposing **active duties** on trustees (statute only applied to passive uses)
- Courts began calling these arrangements "trusts" with "trustees"

> *"William Holdsworth called it 'perhaps the most important addition that the legislature has ever made to our private law.'"*

### Key Trust Legislation

| Year | Legislation | Change |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1535 | Statute of Uses | Attempted to abolish uses |
| 1540 | Statute of Wills | Allowed testamentary disposition |
| 1837 | Wills Act | Modern will requirements |
| 1893 | Trustee Act | Trustee powers and duties |
| 1925 | Trustee Act | Major reform of trustee law |
| 1925 | Law of Property Act | Trusts of land |
| 2000 | Trustee Act | Investment powers modernised |

### Trust Taxation History

Trusts have complex, evolving tax treatment:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| Pre-2006 | Different IHT treatment for IIP, A&M, and discretionary trusts |
| **22 March 2006** | Finance Act 2006 - sweeping changes without consultation |
| 2006 onwards | Nearly all lifetime trusts brought into "relevant property regime" |
| 2008 | A&M trusts brought into regime if not amended by 6 Oct 2008 |
| 2014 | Income held >5 years treated as relevant property |

#### Finance Act 2006 - The Watershed

> *"22 March 2006 was the day of the 2006 Budget which, without any warning or consultation, made sweeping changes to the IHT treatment of trusts."*

**Key Changes:**
- All lifetime trusts post-21 March 2006 are immediately chargeable to IHT
- All such trusts are "relevant property trusts"
- Entry charge: 20% on value exceeding nil-rate band
- 10-yearly periodic charge: up to 6%
- Exit charges on distributions

**Trusts Outside Relevant Property Regime:**
- Bare trusts
- Disabled trusts
- Pre-22 March 2006 IIP trusts (with transitional rules)
- Certain life policy trusts

## NATIONAL INSURANCE HISTORY

### Origin: National Insurance Act 1911

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 16 December 1911 |
| **Effective** | 1 July 1912 (contributions); 15 January 1913 (payments) |
| **Champion** | David Lloyd George (Chancellor) |
| **Model** | German social insurance (visited 1908) |
| **Coverage** | Wage earners <£160/year (~70% of workforce) |

#### Contribution Structure (1911)

| Contributor | Amount |
|-------------|--------|
| Employee | 4d per week |
| Employer | 3d per week |
| State | 2d per week |

> *Lloyd George called it "ninepence for fourpence"*

### Evolution of NI

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1911 | Original Act - health & unemployment insurance |
| 1942 | Beveridge Report - recommended comprehensive reform |
| 1946 | National Insurance Act - universal coverage |
| 1948 | NHS established (separate from NI) |
| 1975 | Social Security Act - Class 2 & 4 for self-employed |
| 2022 | Health and Social Care Levy (introduced then scrapped) |
