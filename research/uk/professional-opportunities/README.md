# UK professional tax opportunities

This directory maps tax work where trained professional judgment can lawfully create value:
finding the right rule, preserving a short deadline, proving a technical fact, correcting an
account, or using the right challenge route.

It does not sell claims. It does not estimate likely fees, revenue, success, damages or payout. It
does not rank professionals or regulators. An opportunity here means **work worth competent
assessment**, not a lead and not money owed.

The canonical data is
[`data/uk-professional-opportunities.json`](data/uk-professional-opportunities.json). It contains:

- 9 professional-opportunity workflows;
- 5 institution-level scrutiny records;
- 67 official, judicial, legislative, regulatory or professional-register sources;
- a shared evidence, deadline, money and challenge workflow; and
- a current boundary for professional status and phased HMRC adviser registration.

The corpus is read-only research. It has no public intake, matching, marketplace, filing,
submission or payment endpoint. Private evidence stays local or in a separately authorised
confidential system.

## The nine areas

| Area | Where specialist judgment matters | Immediate danger |
|---|---|---|
| Business rates valuation | Property state, material day, comparables, Check, Challenge and tribunal evidence | A legal-decision Check for the 2023 list can have a 30 September 2026 deadline; other route clocks differ |
| Commercial-property fixtures | Asset identification, historic pooling, valuation and section 198 drafting | A joint election is normally needed within two years of the transaction |
| Customs repayment and classification | Commodity code, origin, customs value, declaration reconstruction and import-VAT route | The common three-year period is not universal; rejected-goods and withdrawal routes can be shorter |
| Discovery assessments | Discovery power, statutory conditions, behaviour, time limit, quantum and postponement | Appeal and postponement commonly need action within 30 days |
| HMRC decisions and automated penalties | Decision validity, reasonable excuse, account correction, review, tribunal, complaint and direct costs | A complaint does not stop the usual 30-day appeal clock |
| Schedule 36 notices | Reasonable requirement, statutory records, appealability, privilege and controlled production | Appeal and privilege procedures can contain short, different clocks |
| Off-payroll disagreement | Actual working practice, legal status factors, fee-payer chain and payroll | The client has 45 days to respond, but later tax decisions have separate appeal clocks |
| R&D evidence and enquiries | Scheme selection, competent-professional evidence, cost nexus and filing validity | Claim notification can be due six months after the period of account |
| VAT section 80 correction | Error route, net VAT, unjust enrichment and customer reimbursement | The common correction period is four years; review and appeal clocks can be 30 days |

Every deadline in the corpus is a warning to check the actual enactment, notice, territory and
facts. It is not a universal deadline.

## What the money fields mean

Each workflow uses the same sequence:

1. `amountAffected` — the gross amount touched by the issue;
2. `qualifyingBase` — the part supported by the relevant legal conditions;
3. `amountClaimed` — what is actually requested through the proper route;
4. `amountAccepted` — what a decision, agreement or final outcome accepts;
5. `amountReceivedOrCredited` — cash received or a usable credit actually posted;
6. `amountPaidByClient` — tax, reimbursement or another amount actually paid by the client;
7. `redress` — a separate discretionary complaint outcome;
8. `costs` — costs actually incurred;
9. `interestReceived` — interest actually received under an identified legal basis;
10. `interestPaid` — interest actually paid or still due under an identified legal basis;
11. `professionalFees` — agreed invoices actually charged; and
12. `netClientPosition` — the reconciled result after all of the above and any tax effects.

Gross tax, qualifying expenditure and a cancelled decision are not cash. A repayment is not
damages. Reimbursement of reasonable costs is not an award of every fee incurred.

## Professional status is checked, not assumed

Tax advice in the UK is not generally one universally reserved, licensed activity. That does not
mean anyone is suitable for every task.

Each role can carry several separate gates. A prudent-specialism check applies throughout; legal,
professional-body, registration or platform conditions apply only when the stated task and facts
trigger them. For each matter, verify:

- the exact legal entity and written client authority;
- competence for the tax, evidence and procedural route;
- conflicts and professional indemnity insurance;
- anti-money-laundering supervision where the service is in scope;
- current register status where a solicitor, Chartered Tax Adviser or chartered surveyor title is
  relied on; and
- whether paid interaction with HMRC requires registration under the phased Finance Act 2026
  regime.

HMRC registration is registration, not a universal professional licence and not approval of advice
quality. The current guidance introduces different windows from May 2026 to March 2027. A
business solely conducting qualifying court or tribunal appeal work is within a stated exception;
mixed in-scope work can still require registration. See
[HMRC's registration guidance](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-if-and-when-you-need-to-register-as-a-tax-adviser-with-hmrc)
and
[the exceptions manual](https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/mandatory-tax-adviser-registration/mtar10300).

## Sustainable without a lead market

The public map, sources and blank workflow stay open. A professional can charge under their own
clear engagement for genuine private work: inspection, evidence, calculation, advice,
correspondence and representation within their verified competence and authority.

TaxSorted does not sell the introduction, rank the professional, take a referral cut or hold the
client file. The client chooses whom to instruct. The professional controls the confidential
matter system, fees, insurance, conflicts and retention duties. That keeps professional livelihood
attached to useful judgment rather than privileged access to public rules.

## Regulators should be inspectable too

Accountability here means evidence, routes and correction—not a dossier on an employee and not an
inference about motive.

The five scrutiny records retain four things beside every concern:

- what the official source actually says;
- what it does **not** prove;
- the institution's response or another material counterweight; and
- the correction, complaint, review or appeal route.

The current evidence includes:

- HMRC's own 2025 to 2026 statistics: of 74,738 statutory reviews of automated penalties and
  default surcharges, 26,371 original decisions were upheld, 178 varied and 48,189 cancelled.
  HMRC reported 8,128,750 automated penalties, penalty points and default surcharges issued in the
  year. Those figures do not establish why any individual decision changed.
- An anonymised [Adjudicator case study](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/the-adjudicators-office-annual-report-2026/the-adjudicators-office-annual-report-2025)
  in which HMRC persisted with a mistaken National Minimum Wage notice until an appeal was filed.
  The Adjudicator recommended £200 for worry and distress and reasonable agent and counsel costs
  caused by the error. The same study did not uphold a separate complaint about self-correction.
- Another case in the same report where the Valuation Office missed tribunal deadlines and delayed
  responses. The Valuation Office had already recognised the failures, apologised and identified
  learning, so the Adjudicator did not uphold the complaint.
- The
  [2025 to 2026 Charter Stakeholder Group report](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-annual-report-and-accounts-2025-to-2026/charter-stakeholder-group-2025-to-2026-report),
  which records severe deadline, responsiveness and first-time-resolution concerns from 719
  respondents. It is useful diagnostic evidence, not a representative population survey.
- The [National Audit Office customer-service report](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/hmrc-customer-service/),
  which found that HMRC's evaluation of Self Assessment helpline restrictions did not adequately
  assess customer impacts. The report also records HMRC's view that the trial had not harmed return
  submission or tax payment and that it learned lessons.

The same HMRC annual report records a 91.4% success rate for 2,989 decided appeals in 2025 to 2026
under HMRC's definition. That is a necessary counterweight to any suggestion that cancellation
statistics make a new challenge likely to succeed.

## One workflow, finite decisions

The shared workflow is:

1. preserve the document, evidence and earliest plausible clock;
2. verify authority, conflicts, custody and professional gates;
3. classify tax, territory, decision, power and routes;
4. test facts and law in a source-linked matrix;
5. quantify every money state and the zero or adverse scenario;
6. correct or challenge through the proper route; and
7. reconcile the outcome and retain only redacted institution-level learning.

The only assessment terminal decisions are:

- `declined`;
- `needs-more-evidence`;
- `refer-with-consent`; and
- `suitable-for-further-private-review`.

“Suitable” means suitable for a qualified person to assess privately. It does not mean accepted,
filed, likely to succeed or worth a stated amount.

## Routes that must stay separate

The shared public guide presents three source-resolved forks:

- an appeal or statutory review for an appealable tax decision, assessment or penalty;
- a service complaint followed, where eligible, by the Adjudicator and PHSO; and
- judicial review in England and Wales for public-law legality where specialist advice confirms
  the route is appropriate.

Within the first fork, correction, statutory appeal, statutory review and tribunal remain distinct.
Opportunity records retain their more specific correction and challenge routes. Scotland and
Northern Ireland have separate judicial-review court rules.

Running one route must never be allowed to consume another route's deadline.

## Corrections and release

The dataset version is `2026-07-24.3` and its law-as-at date is 24 July 2026. Sources were
retrieved on that date.

The checked-in publication decision binds version `2026-07-24.3`, its exact canonical digest and
all nine IDs, but its status is `pending-qualified-review`. It does not approve publication. Any
corpus edit also fails the public gate closed until a new decision records the new digest.
An approval also needs a completed, named qualified-review record: reviewer and capacity, completion
date, evidence reference, a recorded institutional right-of-reply disposition, and five true
confirmations covering current law and territory, privacy and threat review, absence of intake or a
marketplace, an exercised emergency-stop drill, and assigned correction and withdrawal owners.
Review cannot predate source retrieval, and the decision cannot predate the review. Names, bases and
evidence references in this checked-in file must be public-safe; never put contact details, client
facts or private evidence there. The current record leaves those fields empty and false; changing
only the approval status cannot open either deployment. Production also needs its explicit serving
switch. The public switches stay off until an accountable, appropriately qualified UK reviewer has
completed that record. Public corrections can be reported through the
[TaxSorted issue tracker](https://github.com/cambridgetcg/taxsorted.io/issues). Do not put private
tax evidence in a public issue.
