# UK Window Tax: evidence and media pilot

**Last reviewed:** 16 July 2026
**Status:** First release implemented with three local media files; production publication remains governed by the release workflow
**Confidence:** High for the cited legal chronology and reuse findings; historical causation remains qualified where the sources are qualified

This is the canonical research home for a TaxSorted Window Tax story. It answers
two separate questions:

1. What does the historical evidence support?
2. May TaxSorted lawfully and accurately republish the material used to tell it?

Those questions must never be collapsed. An openly licensed photograph can carry
an unsupported caption. An authoritative archive record can still require
permission before its image is republished.

The candidate-by-candidate record is
[`materials.json`](materials.json). The three first-release assets now have
local production paths, source and rendition hashes, dimensions, rights checks
and change notices. Every other external asset remains pathless until the same
conditions are met.

## Decision

There is enough good material for a first story without asking an archive for
permission:

- a public-domain 1850 `Punch` cartoon from Heidelberg University Library;
- a National Records of Scotland tax-roll extract that expressly records
  windows closed since the previous survey;
- a CC BY-SA photograph of 3 Edgar Street, paired with Historic England's
  qualified conclusion that its alteration was "presumably" made to reduce the
  tax;
- Open Parliament Licence Hansard text showing petitions, disagreement and the
  failed 77-80 repeal vote;
- TaxSorted-authored diagrams and timeline text built from primary sources; and
- The National Archives' written transcript, with its audio held behind one
  final asset-specific rights check.

The National Archives receipt scan, a Historic England archive map and several
museum images remain useful research leads, but they should not be copied into
the product until their separate permissions are settled.

## The short truthful story

The first law was not simply "a tax on every window".

- The statute charged occupied dwellings in England, Wales and
  Berwick-upon-Tweed.
- Its stated purpose was to make good losses created by recoining clipped silver.
- The wider fiscal setting included William III's war with France.
- The occupier was initially liable.
- The first annual bands were `2s` for 0-9 windows, `6s` total for 10-19 and
  `10s` total for 20 or more.
- Later law changed the thresholds and calculation. The 1747 Act brought
  Scotland into the assessed system; surviving schedules begin in 1747/8 and
  National Records of Scotland describes collection from 1748.
- Stopping up windows was real enough for the 1747 legislation to recognise it,
  but a blocked opening in a wall does not by itself prove tax avoidance.
- Doctors, builders, local health bodies and campaigners argued that the duty
  worsened dark and poorly ventilated housing. Government speakers disputed
  parts of that evidence in 1845.
- A repeal resolution failed 77-80 in April 1850.
- The 1851 Act ended the window-count duty and replaced it with an inhabited
  house duty based on annual value. It changed the proxy rather than abandoning
  property taxation.

## Chronology

| Date | Supported event | Important boundary |
|---|---|---|
| 25 March 1696 | The first statute states that the annual charge begins from this date for seven years | Royal Assent was recorded later, on 10 April 1696 |
| 10 April 1696 | Royal Assent to 7 & 8 Will. III c.18, now labelled `Taxation (No. 3) Act 1695` by legislation.gov.uk | The 1695/1696 difference is historical session dating, not evidence of two separate taxes |
| 1696 | Initial bands were 2s for 0-9 windows, 6s for 10-19 and 10s for 20+; the occupier was liable | It was not initially a charge on each individual window |
| 1747/8 | 20 Geo. II c.3 recast the charge, recognised stopping-up as avoidance and brought Scotland into the assessed system; surviving Scottish schedules begin in 1747/8 | Later rates must not be projected backwards onto 1696 |
| 5 April 1825 | Dwellings with no more than seven windows ceased to pay in England, Wales and Berwick; Scotland followed from 24 May | Eight windows became the lowest charged band at that point |
| 18 March 1845 | Commons heard detailed health and building claims together with a government rebuttal | Parliamentary testimony is evidence of the dispute, not proof that every example was correct |
| 9 April 1850 | A repeal resolution failed by three votes, 77-80 | The debate contains a retrospective error about the original top charge |
| 24 July 1851 | 14 & 15 Vict. c.36 received Royal Assent and substituted value-based inhabited house duty | The statutory cessation applied from earlier dates: 5 April in England, Wales and Berwick, and Whitsunday in Scotland |

## Intention and context

The original Act is unusually direct about its immediate legal purpose: receipts
were for the deficiency created when clipped silver was recoined. Parliament's
historical account places the measure within severe wartime financial pressure.

Both statements can be retained without turning one into the other:

- **Stated statutory purpose:** meet the clipped-money deficiency.
- **Wider fiscal context:** a state financing war with France.

The common explanation that windows were selected as a progressive wealth proxy
is plausible and appears in later official interpretation, but it is not stated
as the design rationale in the 1696 preamble. Present it as interpretation, not
as quoted legislative intent.

## Story panels

### 1. A clipped coin, not a window

Open with the original long title and a plain explanation of clipped silver.
Use a TaxSorted-authored diagram rather than an unlicensed coin photograph.

```text
clipped silver -> loss during recoinage -> house-and-window charge
```

The diagram is an explanation, not a historical object.

### 2. A visible proxy with cliffs

Show the first bands as three adjacent houses:

```text
0-9 windows       10-19 windows       20+ windows
2 shillings       6 shillings         10 shillings
```

The change at the threshold matters more than decorative arithmetic. It shows
how a visible proxy can create a reason to alter the measured thing.

### 3. The paper trail records behaviour

Use National Records of Scotland `E326/1/32`, page 73. Its transcription records
one, three and five windows "closed since last survey" in three entries. This is
stronger evidence of a behavioural response than a modern photograph of a wall.

The image remains subject to the NRS limit of 20 Crown-copyright digital images
per website, accurate reproduction, a non-misleading context and NRS
acknowledgement.

### 4. Look carefully at the wall

Use the photograph of 3 Edgar Street only with the Historic England wording:
the ground-floor windows were paired to reduce the facade total from 14 to 12,
"presumably to reduce the Window Tax".

Keep `presumably`. The photograph proves the present visible arrangement. The
official list entry supports a probable interpretation. Neither supplies the
owner's recorded intention.

### 5. Health evidence was argued, not magically settled

Place contemporary claims beside the 1845 government rebuttal. A safe summary
is:

> Contemporary doctors, builders, local health bodies and reformers argued that
> the tax encouraged restrictions on light and ventilation and thereby worsened
> unhealthy housing.

Do not turn that record into a modern estimate of deaths caused by the tax.
Crowding, drainage, sanitation, poverty, heating and disease transmission were
intertwined.

### 6. Three votes

Use the 9 April 1850 Commons division as a clear decision point: repeal failed
77-80. Hansard text is reusable under the Open Parliament Licence, with its
required attribution and a warning that archive transcription can contain
typographical errors.

### 7. Repeal was also redesign

End with the 1851 replacement:

```text
number of windows -> annual rental value
```

This creates a useful reflection question without forcing an answer:

> When a proxy changes behaviour and distributes harm badly, should lawmakers
> remove the tax, change the proxy, change the thresholds, or change who bears
> the charge?

## Material contract

Every published material record must carry:

```text
stable material ID and story beat
kind of material
exact item page and source institution
creator, date and archival locator where known
evidence class
what the material supports
what it does not prove
rights status and rights-statement URL
licence name, version and link where applicable
required credit line and change notice
commercial-use and share-alike conditions
source dimensions and local rendition dimensions
retrieval date, rights-review date and next review date
local file path and integrity hash after acquisition
```

The local asset path must remain empty unless reuse is confirmed or a recorded
condition has been satisfied. A screenshot, crop or format conversion does not
remove the source licence.

## Caption and claim rules

- Name what is visible before naming a cause.
- Use `documented`, `probable`, `possible` or `unknown` for building-level
  attribution.
- Put the source's uncertainty in the caption, not in a hidden method page.
- Keep `supports` and the relevant `doesNotProve` limit beside the material.
- State when an image is satire, reconstruction, later photograph or original
  administrative record.
- Record every crop, rotation, colour adjustment and transcription correction.
- Never use generated imagery as historical evidence. A generated illustration
  may explain a mechanism only when visibly labelled as an illustration.

## Accessibility and static delivery

TaxSorted currently has no historical-media component. The frontend is a static
Next.js export with unoptimised images, so production materials should be local
build-time files rather than hotlinks.

The first component should:

- render a semantic `figure` and visible `figcaption`;
- reserve stable width, height and aspect ratio;
- show creator, holder, original item, credit, licence and modifications;
- accept contextual alt text describing the detail relevant to that panel;
- provide a transcription or adjacent `details` block for document scans;
- keep all key facts in text when an image fails;
- use native audio controls, no autoplay and a complete transcript;
- make motion optional, controlled and unnecessary for comprehension; and
- respect `prefers-reduced-motion` in both CSS and JavaScript.

Media credit should remain visible. The existing click-to-reveal `Cited`
component is useful for figures and calculations, but it should not hide a
material's required attribution.

## Known limits

- This is an evidence and material plan, not a complete fiscal history of every
  rate change across England, Wales, Scotland and Ireland.
- Historical debates contain claims, rhetoric and transcription errors. The
  enacted statute controls when they conflict on the original legal rule.
- Current source terms can change. Rights must be rechecked immediately before
  publication.
- NRS permits bounded online image reuse under stated conditions; it is not a
  blanket open licence over the whole service.
- The National Archives transcript and receipt image have different reuse
  boundaries.
- The first release uses three external images. The NRS image is number 1 of the
  maximum 20 NRS Crown-copyright digital images currently used on TaxSorted.
- The public page and deployment manifest are projections of this research
  ledger; this file remains the canonical evidence and rights record.
