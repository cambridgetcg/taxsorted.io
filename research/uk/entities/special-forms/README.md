## 1. COMMUNITY INTEREST COMPANY (CIC)

### Origins

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Introduced** | 6 April 2005 |
| **Legislation** | Companies (Audit, Investigations and Community Enterprise) Act 2004 |
| **Royal Assent** | 8 July 2004 |
| **Policy Driver** | New Labour government; social enterprise agenda |

#### Background

- First new incorporated legal form in the UK in over a century
- Emerged from 2001 proposal by Public Management Foundation ("The Case for the Public Interest Company")
- Influenced by US public benefit corporation model
- Designed for businesses pursuing community benefit rather than private profit

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Community Interest Test** | Activities must benefit the community |
| **Asset Lock** | Assets cannot be distributed except for community benefit |
| **Dividend Cap** | Maximum 35% of distributable profits |
| **Regulator** | Office of the Regulator of Community Interest Companies |
| **Types** | CIC Limited by Shares or CIC Limited by Guarantee |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Pays CT like any company (NOT a charity) |
| **VAT** | Standard VAT rules apply |
| **Business Rates** | May qualify for discretionary relief |
| **Gift Aid** | NOT eligible (not a charity) |

> **Key Point**: CICs are NOT charities and do NOT receive charity tax exemptions. They are taxed as normal companies.

### Growth

- By March 2025: Over 37,000 CICs registered
- Used across all sectors: health, social care, community services, arts

### Sources

- [Wikipedia: Community Interest Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_interest_company)
- [GOV.UK: CIC Guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/community-interest-companies-how-to-form-a-cic)

## 2. CHARITABLE INCORPORATED ORGANISATION (CIO)

### Origins

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Legislative Framework** | Charities Act 2006 (Part 2, Chapter 8) |
| **Implementation** | 2 January 2013 (Regulations came into force) |
| **Available for Registration** | 4 March 2013 (England & Wales) |
| **Scotland (SCIO)** | April 2011 |
| **Consolidation** | Charities Act 2011 |

#### Background

- Created in response to charity sector requests
- Charities wanted corporate status without company law compliance
- 6-year delay between 2006 Act and 2013 implementation
- First CIO conversion: Challenge to Change (4 March 2013)

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Legal Personality** | Separate legal person (can own property, sue, be sued) |
| **Limited Liability** | Members' liability limited or nil |
| **Single Regulator** | Only Charity Commission (not Companies House) |
| **Types** | Foundation CIO (trustees only) or Association CIO (wider membership) |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Exempt on charitable activities |
| **Income Tax** | Exempt on charitable income |
| **Capital Gains Tax** | Exempt |
| **VAT** | Partial exemptions; complex rules |
| **Gift Aid** | Eligible to reclaim tax on donations |
| **Business Rates** | 80% mandatory relief + discretionary |

### CIO vs Charitable Company

| Aspect | CIO | Charitable Company |
|--------|-----|-------------------|
| Regulator | Charity Commission only | CC + Companies House |
| Filing | CC only | Dual filing required |
| Conversion | Simpler | More complex |
| History | Since 2013 | Long-established |

### Sources

- [Wikipedia: Charitable Incorporated Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charitable_incorporated_organisation)
- [Charity Commission: CIO Guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/setting-up-a-charity-cio)

## 3. COOPERATIVES & COMMUNITY BENEFIT SOCIETIES

### Origins

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **First Cooperative** | Ketley's Building Society, Birmingham, 1775 |
| **Rochdale Pioneers** | 1844 (often cited as modern cooperative origin) |
| **First Legislation** | Industrial and Provident Societies Act 1852 |
| **Modern Legislation** | Co-operative and Community Benefit Societies Act 2014 |
| **Founding Father** | Robert Owen (1771-1858) |

#### Background

- Emerged from 18th-19th century poverty, urbanisation, and lack of worker representation
- Robert Owen implemented cooperative principles at New Lanark cotton mills
- Rochdale Pioneers (1844) established the "Rochdale Principles" of cooperation
- Slaney's Act (1852) first legalised cooperatives in Britain

### Types

| Type | Purpose | Charity Status |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Cooperative Society** | Trade for members' benefit | Cannot be charity |
| **Community Benefit Society (BenCom)** | Trade for community benefit | Can be exempt charity |

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Democratic Control** | One member, one vote |
| **Community Shares** | Withdrawable share capital |
| **Asset Lock** | Optional for cooperatives; required for CBS |
| **Regulator** | Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Generally taxable; CBS with charitable status exempt |
| **Interest on Shares** | Tax-deductible for society |
| **Members** | Interest taxable as savings income |

### Key Legislation Timeline

| Year | Legislation | Change |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1852 | Industrial and Provident Societies Act | First legal framework |
| 1862 | Industrial and Provident Societies Act | Consolidated |
| 1893 | Industrial and Provident Societies Act | Further consolidation |
| 1965 | Industrial and Provident Societies Act | Major reform |
| 2014 | Co-operative and Community Benefit Societies Act | Renamed; modernised |

### Sources

- [Wikipedia: Industrial and Provident Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_and_provident_society)
- [Wikipedia: British Cooperative Movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_co-operative_movement)

## 4. BUILDING SOCIETIES

### Origins

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **First Society** | Ketley's Building Society, Birmingham, 1775 |
| **Founder** | Richard Ketley |
| **First Legislation** | Regulation of Benefit Building Societies Act 1836 |
| **Modern Legislation** | Building Societies Act 1986 |

#### Historical Development

- **Terminating Societies** (1775-1980): Members pooled funds; society dissolved when all housed
- **Permanent Societies** (1845+): Metropolitan Equitable was first; accepted savers not wanting to build
- Last terminating society: First Salisbury and District Perfect Thrift (dissolved 1980)

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Mutual Status** | Owned by members (savers and borrowers) |
| **Purpose** | Mortgage lending and savings |
| **Not-for-Profit** | Surpluses benefit members |
| **Conversion** | Can demutualise to PLC (controversial in 1990s) |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Taxable on profits |
| **Interest Deducted** | Historically deducted tax at source (TDSI) |
| **Members' Interest** | Taxable as savings income |

### Key Legislation Timeline

| Year | Legislation | Change |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1836 | Regulation of Benefit Building Societies Act | First recognition |
| 1874 | Building Societies Act | Comprehensive framework |
| 1894 | Building Societies Act | Further regulation |
| 1962 | Building Societies Act | Modernisation |
| 1986 | Building Societies Act | Deregulation; enabled demutualisation |
| 1997 | Building Societies Act | Post-demutualisation amendments |

### Sources

- [BSA: History of Building Societies](https://www.bsa.org.uk/information/consumer-factsheets/general-information/the-history-of-building-societies)

## 5. FRIENDLY SOCIETIES

### Origins

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Ancient Origins** | Greek and Roman burial societies; medieval guilds |
| **UK Growth** | Mid-18th century (Industrial Revolution) |
| **First Legislation** | Rose's Act 1793 (33 Geo. III, c. 54) |
| **Named After** | George Rose, MP |

#### Background

- Provided mutual aid before welfare state
- Offered sickness, death, and unemployment benefits
- Operated as "cloaks" for early trade unions (illegal under Combination Acts)
- 1793 Act was only legal loophole for workers' organisations

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Mutual Basis** | Members contribute for mutual benefit |
| **Services** | Insurance, pensions, savings |
| **Regulator** | FCA (Prudential Regulation Authority for larger societies) |
| **Tax Status** | Some qualify for tax exemptions |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Exempt on life/endowment business meeting conditions |
| **Tax-Exempt Policies** | Qualifying policies tax-free for policyholders |
| **Limits** | Annual premium limits for tax-exempt status |

### Key Legislation Timeline

| Year | Legislation | Change |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1793 | Rose's Act | First recognition; voluntary registration |
| 1819 | Friendly Societies Act | Enhanced regulation |
| 1846 | Friendly Societies Act | Further reform |
| 1875 | Friendly Societies Act | Comprehensive framework |
| 1896 | Friendly Societies Act | Consolidated |
| 1974 | Friendly Societies Act | Modernisation |
| 1992 | Friendly Societies Act | Deregulation; enabled incorporation |

### Sources

- [Wikipedia: Friendly Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendly_society)
- [Shepherds Friendly: History](https://www.shepherdsfriendly.co.uk/resources/the-history-of-friendly-societies/)

## 6. UNINCORPORATED ASSOCIATIONS

### Legal Status

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Legal Personality** | None - not a separate legal entity |
| **Creation** | 2+ people with common purpose (other than profit) |
| **Examples** | Clubs, societies, churches, sports teams |
| **Formality** | Can be very informal |

### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Liable if trading/investment income |
| **Mutuality Principle** | Dealings between members not taxable |
| **Non-Member Trading** | Taxable profit |
| **CASC Status** | Community Amateur Sports Club relief available |

> **Common Misconception**: Many believe unincorporated associations are exempt from Corporation Tax. They are NOT - but the mutuality principle means member-only activities often produce no taxable profit.

### CASC (Community Amateur Sports Club)

| Benefit | Amount |
|---------|--------|
| Trading Income Relief | Up to £50,000 |
| Property Income Relief | Up to £30,000 |
| Gift Aid | Eligible |
| Business Rates | 80% mandatory relief |

### Sources

- [HMRC: CTM41310 - Unincorporated Associations](https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/company-taxation-manual/ctm41310)

## 7. SCOTTISH PARTNERSHIPS

### The Key Difference

| Jurisdiction | Legal Personality |
|--------------|-------------------|
| **England & Wales** | NO separate personality - partnership = collection of partners |
| **Scotland** | YES separate personality - partnership is distinct from partners |

**Source**: Partnership Act 1890, Section 4(2):
> *"In Scotland a firm is a legal person distinct from the partners of whom it is composed..."*

### Practical Implications

| Aspect | English Partnership | Scottish Partnership |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Property Ownership | Held by partners | Held by partnership |
| Contracts | Partners contract | Partnership contracts |
| Borrowing | In partners' names | In partnership name |
| Insolvency | Partners sequestrated | Partnership + partners separately |
| Membership of Other Entities | Partners join | Partnership can join |

### Tax Treatment

| Aspect | Treatment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Tax Transparency** | Same as English - look through to partners |
| **No Entity Tax** | Partnership itself not taxed |
| **Partner Taxation** | Each partner taxed on share of profits |

> **Key Point**: Despite having separate legal personality, Scottish partnerships are still tax-transparent like English partnerships.

### Scottish Limited Partnerships (SLPs)

Scottish LPs have been particularly attractive for fund structures due to:
- Separate legal personality (can own assets directly)
- Tax transparency
- Limited liability for limited partners

Recent regulatory scrutiny due to misuse in money laundering led to:
- Partnerships (Prosecution) (Scotland) Act 2013
- Enhanced registration requirements

### Sources

- [Wikipedia: United Kingdom Partnership Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_partnership_law)
- [The Gazette: Partnership Insolvency - Scotland vs England](https://www.thegazette.co.uk/all-notices/content/100114)

## 8. INVESTMENT VEHICLES

### REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Introduced** | 1 January 2007 |
| **Legislation** | Finance Act 2006, Part 4 (now CTA 2010, Part 12) |
| **Purpose** | Tax-efficient property investment |
| **Current Number** | 130+ UK REITs |

#### Tax Treatment

| Tax | Treatment |
|-----|-----------|
| **Corporation Tax** | Exempt on qualifying rental income and property gains |
| **Distribution** | Must distribute 90% of rental profits |
| **Shareholder Tax** | Distributions taxed as property income |
| **Withholding** | Basic rate deducted on distributions to individuals |

#### Key Amendments

| Year | Change |
|------|--------|
| 2007 | Introduction |
| 2012 | Entry charge abolished |
| 2013 | REITs can invest in other REITs |
| 2014 | Listing requirement relaxed (AIM eligible) |

### VCTs (Venture Capital Trusts)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Introduced** | Finance Act 1995 |
| **Purpose** | Encourage investment in small UK companies |
| **First VCT** | Murray VCT (1995/96) |
| **Total Raised** | £11 billion since 1995 |

#### Tax Reliefs

| Relief | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| **Income Tax Relief** | 30% of investment (was 20%, briefly 40%) |
| **Annual Limit** | £200,000 |
| **Holding Period** | 5 years minimum |
| **Dividends** | Tax-free |
| **Capital Gains** | Tax-free on disposal |

#### Key Amendments

| Year | Change |
|------|--------|
| 1995 | Introduction at 20% relief |
| 2004 | Temporarily increased to 40% |
| 2006 | Reduced to 30%; holding period to 5 years |
| 2023 | Extended to April 2035 |

### OEICs & Unit Trusts

| Aspect | OEIC | Unit Trust |
|--------|------|------------|
| **Legal Form** | Company | Trust |
| **Legislation** | OEIC Regs 2001 | Various |
| **Units/Shares** | Shares | Units |
| **Pricing** | Single price | Bid/offer spread |

Both are collective investment schemes with similar tax treatment.

### EIS/SEIS Companies

| Scheme | Introduced | Income Tax Relief | CGT Exemption |
|--------|------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **EIS** | 1994 | 30% | Yes (after 3 years) |
| **SEIS** | 2012 | 50% | Yes (after 3 years) |

> **Note**: EIS/SEIS are tax reliefs for investing in qualifying companies, not separate entity types.
