# Law, safety and accountable tax administration

**Reviewed:** 15 July 2026

**Status:** Research memo, not adopted TaxSorted policy, legal advice or a compliance opinion

**Scope:** UK central tax administration, TaxSorted's current professional tools, and selected
professional standards for solicitors, conveyancers, accountants and tax advisers

## Short answer

Law can be good or bad. Being valid law does not by itself make a rule fair, safe, useful or well
executed.

For TaxSorted, "love" can honestly name a design value: respect each person's dignity and agency,
try to understand their situation, make cooperation possible, and repair harm. It cannot be used
as a legal test, a certification, a substitute for evidence or a reason to hide coercive power.

The operational form is:

> exact authority, plain rules, minimum necessary power, evidence before adverse conclusions,
> meaningful participation, authorised representation, bounded information use, reasons,
> correction, independent challenge, remedy and accountable execution.

This is stronger than saying a law has good intentions. A rule that reads well but cannot be
understood, accessed, challenged, corrected or fairly executed has a safety gap.

## Corrections to the starting idea

The original direction was useful, but five distinctions are necessary.

### 1. Public law is not consent-only

A person can owe tax or be subject to a lawful information requirement without opting in. Calling
that relationship consensual would hide the state's power. Safety instead requires exact legal
authority, necessity, proportionality, notice, participation, representation, review and remedy.

Client work is different. A professional normally needs instructions or proper authority to act,
and consent may be needed for a particular disclosure. An API key, browser account, engagement
letter, HMRC agent appointment and data-protection lawful basis are different facts.

### 2. Better communication is not unrestricted data sharing

Departments should coordinate purpose, definitions, provenance, correction signals, case
ownership and accountability. Personal data should remain narrowly scoped, purpose-bound and
shared only through an exact lawful route.

The safe shorthand is:

> Share understanding widely. Disclose personal facts narrowly.

### 3. Test claims and conduct, not whether a person is "real" or "fake"

An error, disagreement, inconsistency, careless statement, deliberate falsehood, regulatory
finding and final court decision are different states. Consequences attach to proved conduct under
the applicable rule. TaxSorted should never turn one state into a permanent identity label or an
honesty score.

### 4. Experience is evidence of impact, not automatic proof of intent

A person's reported distress, exclusion or friction is real evidence of their experience and must
not be dismissed. It does not by itself prove another person's intention, the external cause or
legal liability. Those questions need their own facts, source force and authorised decision-maker.

### 5. Transparency is not total visibility

Institutions exercising public power should expose authority, method, evidence standards,
decisions, corrections and challenge routes. That does not create a right to publish private tax
affairs, privileged material, home details, personal relationships or a worker dossier.

## Source force

Every statement in this area needs a force label.

| Source type | What it can establish | What it cannot establish by itself |
|---|---|---|
| Legislation | A power, duty, condition, right or legal procedure | How consistently it is executed in practice |
| Judgment | The court's decision and legal reasoning in that case | A universal result for materially different facts |
| Regulatory code | Duties within that regulator's statutory or membership scope | A national rule for everyone doing related work |
| Statutory code | Guidance that specified decision-makers must take into account | A new power, lawful basis or immunity from liability |
| Official policy or guidance | The institution's current position or operating route | Binding law, independent proof or a new appeal right |
| Audit, performance or survey evidence | Observed results, reported experience or systemic risk | The result of every individual case |
| TaxSorted analysis | A visible product inference or design choice | Legal authority or an official determination |

Higher force is not always more useful. Legislation may prove a power while current guidance gives
the usable form or address. Both should travel with their real labels.

## The layered safety test

No single control is enough. Each layer catches a different failure.

| Layer | Question | Evidence TaxSorted should require |
|---|---|---|
| Authority | What exact provision permits or requires the act? | Provision, jurisdiction, version, conditions and decision-maker |
| Purpose | What public or client purpose is being served? | Named purpose, affected people and prohibited secondary uses |
| Necessity | Is each request, fact and disclosure needed? | Field-level need and a less-intrusive-alternative check |
| Evidence | What is fact, report, inference, allegation or finding? | Source, locator, date, provenance and status |
| Notice | Can the person understand what happened and what follows? | Decision, amount, facts, law, date, consequence and route |
| Voice | Can the person answer and use an authorised representative? | Representation scope, submissions, accessibility and support |
| Proportionality | Does the action reflect burden, conduct and circumstances? | Reasons, reasonable-excuse inputs, alternatives and reviewer |
| Fresh review | Can someone outside the original stance reconsider it? | Reviewer, authority, evidence considered and power to change |
| Independence | Can an external body examine the relevant issue? | Exact tribunal or court route and its limits |
| Time safety | Are all clocks and deemed outcomes visible? | Trigger, deadline, exceptions, late route and payment effect |
| Confidentiality | Is disclosure lawful, minimal and controlled? | Gateway or authority, data items, recipients and onward limits |
| Repair | Can error and resulting harm be corrected? | Correction, notification, redress, appeal and withdrawal state |
| Oversight | Can the decision-maker and system also be examined? | Audit, complaint trends, incident evidence and accountable owner |

These questions are TaxSorted's synthesis. They are not a claim that one UK statute contains this
whole list.

## What current UK material shows

### Authority has edges

[CRCA 2005 section 5](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/11/section/5) and
[section 9](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/11/section/9) give HMRC collection,
management and ancillary functions. They do not give a free-standing power to rewrite the tax
scheme. In
[R v IRC, ex parte Wilkinson](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/ld200405/ldjudgmt/jd050505/wilkin-1.htm),
the House of Lords rejected using management discretion to create a concession contrary to
Parliament's scheme.

TaxSorted therefore needs the authority for the particular demand, decision, sanction, disclosure
or remedy. "HMRC manages tax" is not enough.

### The Charter is required, but it is not a universal appeal right

[CRCA 2005 section 16A](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/11/section/16A)
requires Commissioners to prepare, review and publish a Charter and report on performance. The
statutory wording describes standards and values to which HMRC will aspire.

The [HMRC Charter](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-charter/the-hmrc-charter)
promises accuracy, ease, responsiveness, fairness, attention to personal circumstances,
representation, security and correction. HMRC's
[Compliance Professional Standards](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/professional-standards-for-hmrcs-compliance-work/hmrc-professional-standards-for-compliance)
say officers should presume customers are truthful absent contrary evidence, make reasoned and
impartial decisions, retain an audit trail and seek specialist input when needed.

Those are meaningful standards. They are not automatically a cause of action, a stay of payment
or an appeal against every HMRC communication. The applicable tax provision still determines the
legal route.

### Execution and written principle diverge

HMRC's [2025 to 2026 annual report](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-annual-report-and-accounts-2025-to-2026/hmrcs-annual-report-and-accounts-2025-to-2026-executive-summary)
reports improved high-level service measures: 85.1% of adviser attempts handled, average telephone
wait of 12 minutes 35 seconds and 79.4% customer satisfaction.

The separately published
[Charter Stakeholder Group report](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-annual-report-and-accounts-2025-to-2026/charter-stakeholder-group-2025-to-2026-report)
records a much harsher view from its 719 taxpayer and agent respondents:

| Charter area | Average score |
|---|---:|
| Being responsive | 2.80 / 10 |
| Making things easy | 3.25 / 10 |
| Getting things right | 3.97 / 10 |
| Recognising representation | 6.31 / 10 |
| Keeping data secure | 6.86 / 10 |

Reported friction included inconsistent advice, delays, repeated work to correct errors, weak case
tracking and escalation, unequal agent access, digital exclusion and deadline asymmetry. Fewer
than 8% reported a noticeable improvement from the Compliance Professional Standards. The report
also says 81.59% considered HMRC insufficiently accountable to the Charter.

These are different measures with different populations and methods. The stakeholder survey is
diagnostic evidence, not a representative population estimate or a finding about every case. It
must not be used to erase the improved high-level measures, and the high-level measures must not
be used to erase the reported friction.

The annual report also acknowledges that attempts to protect taxpayers from error and fraud
negatively affected some Child Benefit customers and says HMRC apologised and corrected cases.
That is a useful safety pattern: name the harm, stop or change the process, repair individual
cases, and learn. The summary does not establish the exact mechanism or every affected case.

### Reasons and routes are decision-specific

There is no safe universal claim that every original HMRC notice must contain full reasons or that
every communication can be appealed.

For example,
[TMA 1970 section 49E](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1970/9/section/49E)
requires a statutory review conclusion and reasoning, while other notice requirements differ by
regime. Information-notice appeal rights under
[Finance Act 2008 Schedule 36](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2008/9/schedule/36)
also contain important limits and exclusions.

[Statutory review](https://www.gov.uk/tax-appeals/review-of-a-tax-or-penalty-decision),
[tribunal appeal](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/appeal-to-the-tax-chamber-of-the-first-tier-tribunal-t242/how-to-appeal-to-the-first-tier-tax-tribunal),
[service complaint](https://www.gov.uk/complain-about-hmrc), Adjudicator review and judicial
review are separate processes. A complaint normally does not replace an appeal, pause an appeal
clock or suspend payment.

TaxSorted should model the decision, facts, law, reasoning, amount, notice date, deadline rule,
payment effect, review route, appeal route and complaint route as separate fields. Missing reasons
stay missing; the product must not invent them.

### Equality includes community, but remains a concrete duty

[Equality Act 2010 section 149](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/15/section/149)
requires public authorities in scope to have due regard to eliminating prohibited discrimination,
advancing equality of opportunity and fostering good relations. HMRC describes its own statutory
responsibilities and current objectives on its
[equality and diversity page](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/hm-revenue-customs/about/equality-and-diversity).

This is the closest binding public-law expression in this review to facilitating community. It
still requires evidence about who is affected and how. "Community" cannot be used to average away
disproportionate harm, accessibility needs or a protected group's experience.

### Good administration includes repair

The [Civil Service Code](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/civil-service-code/the-civil-service-code)
requires integrity, honesty, objectivity and impartiality. Its operational expectations include
accurate records, truthful facts, prompt correction, evidence-based advice, attention to
inconvenient facts and fair treatment.

The Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman's
[Principles of Good Administration](https://www.ombudsman.org.uk/about-us/our-principles/principles-good-administration)
group good administration into getting it right, customer focus, openness and accountability,
fairness and proportionality, putting things right and continuous improvement.

These are standards and evaluative principles, not a new tax appeal route. They reinforce that
execution and repair belong inside the safety model.

## Communication between departments

### Binding boundaries

[CRCA 2005 sections 18 to 20](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2005/11/pdfs/ukpga_20050011_en.pdf)
make HMRC information confidential by default, provide specific exceptions and control public
interest and onward disclosures. Wrongful disclosure of identifiable revenue information can be
criminal. A generally useful purpose is not itself a gateway.

Part 5 of the
[Digital Economy Act 2017](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2017/30/contents)
provides defined, permissive sharing gateways for specified public-service, debt and fraud
purposes. It is not a universal sharing power. The associated
[statutory code](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/digital-economy-act-2017-part-5-codes-of-practice/code-of-practice-for-public-authorities-disclosing-information-under-chapters-1-3-and-4-public-service-delivery-debt-and-fraud-of-part-5-of-the-di)
favours minimum necessary data, audit trails, impact assessment and purpose limits. It recommends
attribute or binary API checks where full records are unnecessary and says linked data should not
be used to create a new identity register.

[DUAA 2025 section 123](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/section/123), fully commenced
on 12 February 2026 by
[SI 2026/126](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/126/regulation/3/made), expanded the criteria
for a public-service-delivery objective so it may assist undertakings with a trade, business or
charitable purpose. It did not itself create such an objective, make TaxSorted a specified person
or supply a universal gateway. The 2020 statutory code predates this amendment, so its older
individual-and-household wording is not a complete statement of current scope.

Under the UK GDPR and Part 2 of the
[Data Protection Act 2018](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/12/contents),
as amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, the general processing principles continue to
require lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, minimisation, accuracy, storage
limitation, security and accountability. The ICO's
[current principles guide](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/data-protection-principles/a-guide-to-the-data-protection-principles/)
explains those duties. Compatibility with a later purpose is not a lawful basis or disclosure
gateway.

The regimes are not interchangeable. Part 3 of the DPA 2018 has separate law-enforcement
principles: fairness applies, but transparency is not listed as a principle because disclosure can
prejudice an investigation; section 44 instead governs information made available to data
subjects. The ICO's
[current explanation](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/data-sharing/data-sharing-a-code-of-practice/fairness-and-transparency-in-data-sharing/)
sets out that distinction. Every connector must identify whether UK GDPR and Part 2, Part 3 or,
where relevant, Part 4 applies before choosing controls or making a compliance claim.

The [ICO Data Sharing Code](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/data-sharing/data-sharing-a-code-of-practice/about-this-code/)
is a statutory code containing both legal explanation and optional good practice. Some pages are
under review following the 2025 Act, so old summaries of automated-decision law must not be copied
as current law.

For data-protection complaints received on or after 19 June 2026, current duties commenced through
[SI 2026/82](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/82/pdfs/uksi_20260082_en.pdf).
The ICO's
[complaints guidance](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/how-to-deal-with-data-protection-complaints/)
calls for a clear route, acknowledgement within 30 days, appropriate investigation, progress
information and an outcome without undue delay. That is a concrete floor for TaxSorted's future
rights and correction intake, not proof that the intake exists today.

### Safe coupling

| Couple across teams | Keep narrow and purpose-bound |
|---|---|
| Definitions and data standards | Personal and privileged facts |
| Named owner and escalation contact | Identifiers usable across every service |
| Legal-authority and impact-assessment metadata | Bulk copies of source systems |
| Source, provenance and quality status | Access outside the recipient's need |
| Correction, withdrawal and revocation events | Onward disclosure and secondary use |
| Decision record and independent audit evidence | Retention after the purpose ends |

One team may coordinate a joined-up response while every contribution retains its source,
authority, purpose and correction path. Collection, decision, enforcement and audit should not
collapse into one unreviewable role.

### Automated decisions and algorithmic transparency

[DUAA 2025 section 80](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/18/section/80)
replaced the prior UK GDPR automated-decision rule with Articles 22A to 22D and also changed the
Part 3 and Part 4 DPA regimes. The relevant provisions commenced on 5 February 2026 through
[SI 2026/82](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/82/pdfs/uksi_20260082_en.pdf).
Significant solely automated decisions using non-special-category data are now permitted more
broadly, but safeguards include information, representations, meaningful human intervention and a
route to contest. Special-category data remains more restricted, and Article 22B(4) prohibits a
significant solely automated decision where its processing relies wholly or partly on the new
Article 6(1)(ea) recognised-legitimate-interests basis. Under
[regulation 5](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/82/regulation/5/made), the previous regime
is preserved for relevant decisions taken before 5 February 2026, so the decision date is part of
the legal test. A nominal reviewer without evidence, competence or power to change the result is
not meaningful human intervention.

The
[Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/algorithmic-transparency-recording-standard-mandatory-scope-and-exemptions-policy/algorithmic-transparency-recording-standard-atrs-mandatory-scope-and-exemptions-policy)
is mandatory central-government policy for relevant tools, not an Act. TaxSorted is not itself
within that mandate merely because it is software. A public authority deploying it may be.

Current HMRC records show useful patterns. The
[VAT Return Analysis Tool](https://www.gov.uk/algorithmic-transparency-records/hmrc-vat-return-analysis-tool)
is described as supporting officer scrutiny rather than making the decision; its record names the
human role, ordinary review and appeal route, access logs, source data and impact assessment. The
[Organisational Listening Tool](https://www.gov.uk/algorithmic-transparency-records/organisational-listening-tool)
states its source, models, access group, two-year retention, personal-data redaction and impact
assessment. Publication does not prove perfect operation, but it gives an affected community a
more inspectable claim.

TaxSorted should publish an ATRS-like record for every consequential calculator or indicator even
where it is not legally required: purpose, owner, source data, logic, version, limitations, human
role, testing, correction route and stop condition.

## Professional duties converge

The selected frameworks apply to different people. They must not be merged into a fictional
national licence.

| Framework | Force and scope | Relevant duties | Does not prove |
|---|---|---|---|
| [SRA Principles](https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/principles/) and [Code](https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/code-conduct-solicitors/) | Regulatory duties for SRA-regulated people and firms | Rule of law, public trust, independence, honesty, integrity, client interests, authority, confidentiality, competence and correction | That every lawyer or tax adviser is SRA-regulated |
| [CLC Code](https://www.clc-uk.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/Code-of-ConductV1.0Jan2025.pdf) | Regulatory arrangements for CLC-regulated people and bodies | Honest service, useful information, instructions, conflicts, confidentiality and redress | A rule for work outside CLC scope |
| [ICAEW Code of Ethics 2026](https://www.icaew.com/technical/trust-and-ethics/ethics/code-of-ethics) | Binding professional code for people and firms within its stated scope from 1 July 2026 | Integrity, objectivity, competence, due care, confidentiality, behaviour and responsible technology use | Statutory authorisation to provide every service |
| [PCRT 2026](https://www.tax.org.uk/professional-conduct-in-relation-to-taxation-pcrt) | Membership standard for members, students and affiliates within seven bodies' stated scopes | Tax-specific integrity, objectivity, competence, confidentiality, professional behaviour and planning standards | Legislation, a duty for non-members or binding force for every linked help-sheet step |
| [HMRC Standard for Agents](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/hmrc-the-standard-for-agents/the-hmrc-standard-for-agents) | HMRC guidance and expectations for agents dealing with HMRC | Honest identity and claims, current competence, evidence, records, client correction and no credential sharing | Replacement of law, privilege or professional duties |

Across these scopes, the recurring operational duties are:

- understand the client and the exact instruction;
- act only with proper authority;
- protect confidential information and preserve privilege only where it applies to the exact material;
- identify conflicts and improper influence;
- work within current competence and obtain specialist help;
- expose uncertainty and do not over-rely on technology;
- keep a contemporaneous record;
- correct errors and explain their impact where the applicable framework requires it;
- escalate, cease or withdraw only under the applicable source, trigger and retainer terms; and
- remain accountable for the professional decision.

These are converging concerns, not one universal procedure. PCRT's detailed correction and
cessation material is non-mandatory help-sheet guidance, while specific planning rules require a
member to consider withdrawal. The SRA's
[retainer guidance](https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/guidance/terminating-retainer/) requires a
case-specific basis, reasonable notice, lawful termination and mitigation. Withdrawal does not
itself authorise disclosing the client's underlying error. A legal duty such as an
anti-money-laundering report needs its own assessment. "More communication" must never skip that
boundary.

Confidentiality is also not the same as legal professional privilege. In
[*Prudential* (2013) UKSC 1](https://supremecourt.uk/cases/uksc-2010-0215), the Supreme Court
majority did not extend legal advice privilege to tax advice given by non-lawyer accountants.
Privilege must be established for the exact material, adviser, purpose and jurisdiction; neither
professional status nor a TaxSorted `confidential` label creates it.

## Claims and people

TaxSorted should use a claim-state model rather than a person score.

| State | Meaning | Permitted conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| `unknown` | Required evidence has not been obtained | No positive or negative conclusion |
| `reported` | A named source or person made the statement | The report exists, not that it is externally true |
| `source-supported` | Admitted evidence supports the narrow claim | Support within the stated source and date |
| `disputed` | A relevant party or source gives a conflicting position | The disagreement and each side's evidence |
| `contradicted` | Admitted evidence conflicts with the claim | The precise contradiction, not the speaker's identity |
| `corrected` | The claim or record has been amended | Prior and current version plus effect of correction |
| `official-finding` | An authorised body made a finding | Exact finding, status, scope and challenge route |
| `under-appeal` | The finding is being challenged | Finding remains separate from finality |
| `ordinary-deadline-passed` | The ordinary time limit appears to have expired | No claim of finality; record any extension, out-of-time, set-aside or reopening route as available, unavailable or unknown |
| `final-for-route` | Evidence shows the named route is exhausted, including the status of any late or reopening route | Finality only for the named route, jurisdiction and recorded date |

Where a statute distinguishes reasonable care, careless conduct, deliberate conduct and
concealment, TaxSorted should preserve those exact categories and burdens. A risk signal,
inconsistency or missing document is never itself a finding of dishonesty.

## Law as a shared reflection object

TaxSorted can help every reader ask whether a law is constructive, whether it should change and
what evidence would justify that change. Its role is to make the law, its stated purposes, its
operation, its effects, the disagreement and the available routes inspectable. It should not issue
one moral score, run a popularity contest or present itself as an official consultation unless a
competent public body has expressly made it one.

The product contract is:

> TaxSorted helps people distinguish what the law currently says, why its authors said it was
> needed, what was predicted, what happened in practice, what people experienced and what changes
> they propose. Participation is evidence for understanding, not a vote on truth.

### Keep six lanes separate

| Lane | What it contains | What it does not prove |
|---|---|---|
| Current or proposed law | Exact provision, jurisdiction, force, status, version, commencement and amendments | That the rule is fair, effective or well executed |
| Stated purposes | Attributed consultation papers, explanatory material, impact material and statements | One collective or hidden mental intention |
| Expected effects | Published forecasts, costs, benefits, risks, distribution and evaluation measures | What later happened |
| Observed effects | Audits, statistics, research, judgments, administration evidence and scoped experience reports | One cause, universal prevalence or another person's motive |
| Reflection | Questions and value judgments about dignity, fairness, proportionality, clarity and constructiveness | Legal invalidity or representative public opinion |
| Proposed change | Attributed case to retain, amend, simplify, replace, review or repeal | Adoption, authority or implementation |

The current
[Guide to Making Legislation](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/guide-to-making-legislation/guide-to-making-legislation-html--2)
says explanatory notes should make a Bill accessible and explain its context and practical
operation. It also says they are prepared by the sponsoring department, are not legislation, are
not endorsed by Parliament and are not an authoritative interpretation. TaxSorted should therefore
publish `stated purposes`, plural, with speaker or publisher, date, source force and scope. A
consultation paper, explanatory note, impact assessment, ministerial statement, vote and observed
effect are different evidence.

There is a specific tax scrutiny opportunity. The same guide normally expects a departmental
memorandum three to five years after Royal Assent comparing an Act's operation with its objectives,
but Finance Acts and tax-law rewrite Acts are exempt from the automatic memorandum requirement.
TaxSorted can preserve measure-level objectives, forecasts, implementation evidence and later
effects without pretending to replace Parliamentary, governmental or independent review.

### Turn "is it honest?" into observable questions

A law does not itself have a mind. A reader may still reasonably ask whether its public account is
honest. TaxSorted should help them examine:

- who stated the problem and purpose, when and in what capacity;
- what material facts, uncertainty, alternatives and counter-evidence were disclosed;
- whether the enacted mechanism fits the stated purpose;
- whether execution and observed effects fit either the mechanism or the prediction;
- whether contradictions, failed predictions and errors were acknowledged and corrected; and
- whether a competent body has made a finding about knowing deception.

The base record may say `unsupported`, `mixed`, `disputed`, `inconsistent with`, `material issue
not addressed` or `unknown`. It should use `dishonest`, `lied` or deliberate-deception language only
where the evidence supports that exact conclusion under a stated standard. Poor design, an adverse
outcome, disagreement and deception are not synonyms.

### Questions for constructive reflection

Each law or proposal should invite the same bounded questions:

1. What problem is the rule meant to address, and what evidence establishes the problem?
2. Who benefits, and who bears money, time, risk, information disclosure or administrative work?
3. Does the mechanism plausibly address the stated problem?
4. Can affected people understand, comply with, question and challenge it?
5. Were simpler, less intrusive or less unequal alternatives considered?
6. What did the sponsor predict, and what would show the policy was not working?
7. What has happened in practice, for whom, over what period and with what evidence limits?
8. Can errors and unintended harm be corrected?
9. Should the exact provision be retained, amended, simplified, evaluated, replaced or repealed?
10. What evidence could change the contributor's present view?

No answer becomes a universal `constructive` score. TaxSorted should show the strongest sourced
case to retain, modify and replace the rule, plus unresolved evidence and affected voices not yet
heard.

### Contribution and synthesis model

A future contribution must identify its kind:

`question`, `experience-report`, `factual-claim`, `legal-interpretation`, `value-judgment`,
`official-purpose-source`, `proposal`, `counterargument` or `correction`.

Kind is separate from publication state. Publication can be `draft`, `submitted`,
`clarification-needed`, `published`, `disputed`, `corrected`, `superseded`,
`temporarily-restricted`, `removed-with-reason` or `archived`. `Approved` is not a publication
state because publication is not TaxSorted endorsement. An experience report proves that a person
reported an experience; it does not automatically prove the cause, prevalence, intention or legal
liability.

The synthesis should be organised by proposition, evidence and trade-off rather than contributor
or posting velocity. It should preserve one material lawful dissenting position even if many more
submissions take another view. Repeated campaign text may be grouped, with the distinct evidence
or reasoning retained. The Office for Statistics Regulation's current
[public-involvement toolkit](https://osr.statisticsauthority.gov.uk/guidance/public-involvement-and-engagement-in-official-statistics-a-toolkit-for-statistics-producers/pages/4/)
warns that there is no single "public", supports deliberation where values differ, and says
organisations should be honest about the scope of public influence and show what changed. Every
public count must say:

> These contributions are not a representative poll. A response count does not establish truth,
> public support or consensus.

The first product should remain read-only and local: a sourced law-reflection record plus a
browser-local worksheet or export that helps a person form their own view and take it to the
correct official route. It should not store, publish or rank political views in the first release.
The current
[consultation principles](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/consultation-principles-guidance/consultation-principles-2018)
support clear questions, informed responses, formative-stage engagement, inclusion and explaining
how responses informed policy, but they are guidance without legal force. A TaxSorted reflection
does not become a government consultation or Parliamentary submission merely because it is public.

```text
observedEffects[]
unintendedEffects[]
administrativeBurdens[]
changeProposals[]
reflectionQuestions[]
officialParticipationRoutes[]
knownGaps[], corrections[], reviewedOn
```

Each stated purpose and effect needs its source voice, date, force, scope, `supports` and
`doesNotProve`. Each change proposal needs the exact provision, proposer, requested change,
benefits, burdens, alternatives, transition, evaluation and status. The first API is `GET` only.

### Gates before public contributions

Open comments, replies, profiles, likes, follower graphs and engagement ranking are not the first
release. Before TaxSorted hosts user material it needs:

- a privacy, minimisation, special-category-data and retention assessment;
- jurisdiction-specific confidentiality and defamation review;
- clear moderation, reporting, complaint, correction and appeal rules;
- human capacity to review threats, private tax facts and named-person allegations before
  publication;
- correction propagation across pages, exports, syntheses and caches;
- rate limits that do not become contributor identity or reputation scores;
- an accountable safety owner, conflict recusal and an exercised contribution stop; and
- an Online Safety Act scope assessment, illegal-content risk assessment and child-access
  assessment where applicable.

Ofcom's current
[regulation checker](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/os-toolkit/regulation-checker/regulation-checker)
lists discussion forums and information-sharing or review services among possible user-to-user
services. Its
[illegal-content guidance](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/online-safety/illegal-and-harmful-content/illegal-content-duties-under-the-online-safety-act)
describes current risk-assessment, moderation, reporting, complaint, record and review duties for
services in scope. TaxSorted should determine the exact scope before launch, not infer an exemption
from small size or good intent.

Every reflection ends at a real route: a current
[tax consultation](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-the-status-of-tax-policy-consultations),
[Public Bill Committee](https://www.parliament.uk/mps-lords-and-offices/offices/commons/scrutinyunit/written-submissions/),
[select committee inquiry](https://committees.parliament.uk/inquiries/?currentlyacceptingevidence=true&showadvanced=true),
[Budget representation](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/budget-representations-guidance/guidance-for-submitting-your-budget-or-autumn-statement-representation),
representative,
[petition](https://petition.parliament.uk/help),
[Law Commission reform idea](https://lawcom.gov.uk/ideas_for_law_reform/) or other competent
process. Individual tax disputes continue to use their separate review, appeal and complaint paths.

## TaxSorted today

### Existing alignment

TaxSorted already has unusually strong foundations:

- [TaxSorted's principles](../../../PRINCIPLES.md) already say rules should be understandable,
  education should be open and accountants should be able to press for rules that make no sense to
  be removed.
- The [tax-system method](../tax-system/METHOD.md) labels source force, records what a source does
  not prove, exposes conflicts and refuses guessed joins.
- The current tax-system frameworks already separate public rationale, operation and tension, and
  the live Learn pages explain lawmaking, participation routes and sourced examples of policy
  change.
- The [tax-expert contract](../tax-expert/README.md) separates facts, unknowns, rules, source force,
  reasoning, capability, review stops and action ownership.
- The shared why graph keeps the caller, duty holder, administrator and official decision-maker
  separate, and refuses to invent an appeal route.
- The professional manifest names bounded executable jobs, facts not to send, review stops,
  missing capabilities and the firm's duty to preserve its matter evidence.
- The [observer-accountability framework](../observer-accountability/README.md) applies review,
  complaint, appeal, audit and oversight to the observer too.
- The [Public Data Charter](../../../docs/PUBLIC-DATA-CHARTER.md) already says smaller truth wins,
  public availability is not permission to republish everything, and allegations, findings,
  sanctions, appeals and corrections must remain distinct.
- High-risk candidate datasets remain empty when correction intake, controller decisions,
  source-rights review, audit evidence and an exercised stop are absent.

### Current gaps

The value is scattered across several documents and is not yet one adopted, testable product
contract. More importantly, declared intent is ahead of execution in several places:

- The Public Data Charter is an agent-authored draft, not an adopted policy.
- No confidential correction and safety intake is live for high-risk publication.
- The professional service has no published professional privacy and retention policy, security
  assessment, high-availability contract or service level.
- Secure customer credential issuance for external users, self-service credential lifecycle and
  an authenticated operator audit trail are not live.
- TaxSorted returns bounded outputs but does not provide a retrievable matter file or a versioned,
  tamper-evident, retention-managed evidence export with legal-hold and deletion rules.
- Client or matter identity, professional roles, conflicts, approval and client authority are not
  modelled in the executable professional tasks.
- Corrections do not yet propagate to every affected professional result with a visible
  `do-not-rely` or withdrawal state.
- There is no connector-level authority manifest covering purpose, controller roles, exact data,
  recipients, retention, onward disclosure and review date.
- Consequential tools do not yet have a common ATRS-like public record.
- There is no per-law reflection contract joining attributed purposes, expected and observed
  effects, consultation history, proposed changes and current official participation routes.
- There is no safe civic-contribution intake or completed Online Safety Act scope assessment. The
  current public feedback route is for TaxSorted wishes and corrections through GitHub, not law
  deliberation.
- A human-review claim is not backed by linked per-review evidence showing the internal actor,
  reviewed object and version, time, evidence, outcome and authority appropriate to the claim.

These gaps do not make the present bounded tools dishonest. The current manifest already states
most of its limits. They do prevent broad claims such as "professional-ready", "compliant",
"confidential", "human-reviewed" or "safe" without qualification.

## Claim guardrails

| Word | Evidence required before TaxSorted uses it without qualification |
|---|---|
| `correct` | Complete admitted facts, applicable ruleset, reproducible result and no material stop |
| `compliant` | Named requirement, jurisdiction, version, scope, assessor and evidence |
| `safe` | Named threat model, tested controls, residual risks, incidents and review date |
| `approved` or `endorsed` | Exact approving body, object, scope, date and current status |
| `recognised` | Exact programme and capability; never general quality or security |
| `human-reviewed` | Internal actor, reviewed object and version, time, evidence, outcome and authority; publish only the role where identity should remain private |
| `confidential` | Purpose, access, encryption, retention, deletion, contracts and incident process; never an implication of legal privilege |
| `integrated` | Exact system, environment, data direction, authority and tested current state |
| `transparent` | Published purpose, logic, evidence, limits, owner, corrections and known omissions |
| `independent` | Governance, appointment, funding, decision and removal boundaries |
| `verified` | Verifier, method, source, time, scope and what was not checked |

When that evidence is absent, use the smaller true phrase: `calculated under ruleset X`,
`source-supported`, `review required`, `implementation present`, `deployment unverified` or
`unknown`.

For a significant solely automated decision, `human-reviewed` is still insufficient unless the
human had meaningful involvement and power to reconsider the result. An editorial review claim
needs evidence that the review happened, but not necessarily decision-changing authority.

## Research-backed product contract

The public value statement should remain short. The machinery underneath it must be exact.

### Candidate public wording

This is research copy, not a change to the live site:

> TaxSorted provides sourced UK tax data, bounded calculations and workflow evidence. It shows
> the facts, assumptions, source force, limits, decision owner and available challenge route. It
> does not decide who is trustworthy, replace professional judgement or act without recorded
> authority.

The project value may be stated separately:

> We believe tax law and its execution should help people understand their duties, participate
> with dignity, work together and repair mistakes. We test that belief through evidence,
> boundaries, correction and challenge.

### Decision record

Every consequential answer should be able to expose:

```text
decision type and effect
jurisdiction and relevant period
decision-maker and legal authority
facts used, unknowns and disputed facts
binding law, guidance and TaxSorted inference
reasoning and calculation version
notice date and every relevant clock
client authority and accountable professional action
review, appeal, complaint and payment effects
correction, withdrawal and do-not-rely state
```

### Department or connector handoff

Every handoff involving non-public data should record:

```text
sender, recipient and controller, joint-controller or processor roles
applicable data-protection regime
exact purpose and statutory disclosure gateway
Article 6 lawful basis
Article 9 condition or Article 10 and DPA authority where relevant
minimum data or attribute disclosed
source, quality, dispute and correction status
access, retention and onward-disclosure limits
notice and rights route where required
linked impact assessment, sharing agreement and code or register evidence where required
owner, review date and audit event
```

An API key proves technical access only. It never proves client authority, consent, professional
status, lawful basis or a statutory gateway.

### Correction record

A correction needs the previous and current claim, reason, source, time, reviewer, affected output
IDs, downstream recipients, notification state and whether old outputs may still be relied on. A
silent overwrite is not a correction system.

## Recommended build order

This research does not recommend changing public copy first.

1. Ask Yu to adopt or amend one short law-and-safety charter with the distinctions in this memo.
2. Add the `law-reflection` contract and one sourced, read-only pilot with a browser-local
   worksheet or export. Do not collect public contributions yet.
3. Build the confidential rights, correction and safety intake, with acknowledgement, triage,
   urgent stop, audit and downstream notification.
4. Publish the professional privacy, retention and security boundaries and add secure key delivery
   plus authenticated operator audit.
5. Add versioned decision, authority-handoff and correction contracts before collecting more
   client or cross-department data.
6. Publish ATRS-like records for consequential tools and current source ledgers for the
   professional codes and data rules used here.
7. Add matter-scoped professional roles, conflicts, client authority, review and evidence-pack
   export without turning TaxSorted into a filing or practice-management system by implication.
8. After the public-contribution gates are evidenced, pilot reviewed submissions for one bounded
   law version with no popularity ranking and an exercised stop.
9. Only then update the wider website claim, keeping execution metrics and unresolved gaps beside
   it.

## Failure modes to test

- A complaint is mistaken for an appeal and the statutory deadline expires.
- A stated 30-day norm is treated as the deadline for every route.
- Internal delay continues while the person's shorter clock runs.
- Silence produces a deemed outcome that the interface did not show.
- A generated notice states an amount without usable facts, law or reasoning.
- Several teams contribute but no one owns resolution.
- A professional cannot see the same relevant state as the represented client.
- A digital-only path excludes someone who needs support or another channel.
- Guidance is stale or presented as enacted law.
- A department's explanatory material is presented as the one true intention of Parliament.
- Discussion attached to an old law version is displayed as if it concerns the current rule.
- A response count is presented as truth, consensus or representative public opinion.
- A TaxSorted reflection is mistaken for an official consultation or Parliamentary submission.
- A public contribution exposes personal tax facts or unsupported allegations about a named person.
- Moderation rewards repetition, suppresses material dissent or ranks contributors by trust.
- A risk score becomes an allegation of dishonesty.
- Automation ignores reasonable excuse, disclosure or personal circumstances.
- Useful-looking data is shared without a gateway or beyond its purpose.
- One incorrect source record propagates through every connected service.
- Privacy is used to hide reasons, or transparency is used to leak tax affairs.
- A reviewer can endorse but cannot inspect evidence or change the outcome.
- A discretionary complaint payment is described as legal compensation.
- A disclaimer says "professional review" but no review event is retained.

## Known limits

- This is product research, not a complete account of UK public, tax, privacy or professional law.
- The first historical-law reflection pilot is researched separately in
  [`../tax-history/window-tax/`](../tax-history/window-tax/). Its material ledger keeps historical
  support, image rights and building-level causation separate; no public story exists yet.
- Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have distinct taxes, institutions and court procedures that
  need route-specific additions.
- The professional frameworks selected here have different scopes; not every lawyer, accountant
  or adviser belongs to one of them.
- Some ICO material is under review after the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025. Release decisions
  must recheck current law and guidance.
- The Charter stakeholder survey is useful diagnostic evidence, not a representative sample.
- Official self-description proves what an institution publishes, not that every practice matches.
- Repository code and tests prove an implementation state, not the live deployment, organisational
  practice, security posture or legal compliance.
- No public law-reflection contribution system exists today. The schema, worksheet, intake and
  safety gates in this memo are recommendations, not implemented capability.
- "Love", "community" and "safety" remain value words unless each public claim points to the
  concrete controls and evidence meant in that context.
